Biology: In Depth Analysis

Comprehensive exploration of life sciences

Introduction to Biology

Biology is the scientific study of life and living organisms. It encompasses an extraordinarily broad range of topics, from the molecular mechanisms within cells to the complex interactions of ecosystems. The field seeks to understand the structure, function, growth, origin, evolution, distribution, and classification of all forms of life.

Biology integrates knowledge from chemistry, physics, mathematics, and other sciences to explain the phenomena of life. Modern biology is characterized by an interdisciplinary approach, combining molecular and cellular biology with genetics, ecology, evolution, and physiology to provide a comprehensive understanding of living systems.

The study of biology has revolutionized our understanding of health, disease, biodiversity, and the environment, leading to advances in medicine, biotechnology, conservation, and agriculture that have transformed human society.

Cell Biology: The Foundation of Life

The cell is the fundamental unit of life. All living organisms are composed of cells, which carry out essential functions through complex molecular machinery. Cell biology examines the structure, function, and behavior of cells.

Cell Theory:

The cell theory, a fundamental principle of biology, states: (1) All living organisms are composed of one or more cells, (2) The cell is the basic unit of life, and (3) All cells arise from pre-existing cells through cell division.

Cell Types:

  • • Prokaryotic Cells: Simple cells lacking a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles (bacteria and archaea). DNA is located in the nucleoid region. Smaller and less complex than eukaryotic cells.
  • • Eukaryotic Cells: Complex cells with a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles (plants, animals, fungi, protists). Larger and more structurally complex, allowing for greater functional specialization.

Cell Organelles and Structures:

  • • Nucleus: The control center of the cell, containing DNA wrapped around histone proteins to form chromatin. Enclosed by a double membrane (nuclear envelope) with pores for RNA and protein transport. Contains the nucleolus, where ribosomal RNA is synthesized. Controls cellular activities through gene expression regulation.
  • • Mitochondria: Powerhouses of the cell, producing ATP through cellular respiration (oxidative phosphorylation). Have their own DNA and ribosomes, suggesting an endosymbiotic origin. Number varies by cell type and energy demands. Double membrane structure with inner membrane folded into cristae, increasing surface area for ATP production.
  • • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): Network of membrane-bound tubules and sacs. Rough ER has ribosomes attached, synthesizing proteins destined for secretion or membrane insertion. Smooth ER lacks ribosomes, synthesizing lipids, metabolizing carbohydrates, detoxifying drugs and poisons, and storing calcium ions (in muscle cells).
  • • Golgi Apparatus: Stack of flattened membrane-bound sacs (cisternae). Modifies, sorts, packages, and distributes proteins and lipids from ER. Has distinct cis (receiving) and trans (shipping) faces. Adds molecular tags (like carbohydrates) to proteins, creating glycoproteins. Packages materials into vesicles for transport to destinations.
  • • Ribosomes: Sites of protein synthesis, composed of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and proteins. Can be free in cytoplasm (synthesizing proteins for cell use) or attached to rough ER (synthesizing proteins for secretion/membranes). Consist of large and small subunits that assemble during translation.
  • • Lysosomes: Membrane-bound organelles containing hydrolytic enzymes that break down macromolecules, cellular debris, and foreign materials. Maintain acidic pH (pH ~4.5) for optimal enzyme activity. Function in intracellular digestion, apoptosis (programmed cell death), and defense against pathogens.
  • • Peroxisomes: Membrane-bound organelles containing enzymes (catalase, peroxidases) that detoxify harmful substances like hydrogen peroxide, break down fatty acids, and participate in lipid metabolism. Produce hydrogen peroxide as a byproduct, which is then broken down by catalase.
  • • Cytoskeleton: Network of protein filaments providing structural support, enabling cell movement, and facilitating intracellular transport. Consists of microtubules (thickest, involved in cell division and intracellular transport), intermediate filaments (structural support), and microfilaments (thinnest, involved in cell movement and shape).
  • • Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane): Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins, forming a selectively permeable barrier. Maintains cell integrity, regulates transport of materials (passive diffusion, facilitated diffusion, active transport), and facilitates cell communication. Contains cholesterol (in animal cells) for fluidity and stability.
  • • Chloroplasts: Plant organelles containing chlorophyll, where photosynthesis occurs. Have their own DNA and ribosomes, suggesting endosymbiotic origin. Double membrane with internal thylakoid membranes organized into grana, where light-dependent reactions occur. Stroma contains enzymes for light-independent (Calvin cycle) reactions.
  • • Vacuoles: Large membrane-bound storage compartments. In plant cells, central vacuole stores water, ions, nutrients, and waste products, maintaining turgor pressure. In animal cells, smaller vacuoles store materials temporarily.

Cell Division:

Cell division is essential for growth, development, tissue repair, and reproduction. Two main types occur in eukaryotes:

  • • Mitosis: Nuclear division producing two genetically identical daughter cells. Consists of phases: Prophase (chromosomes condense, nuclear envelope breaks down), Metaphase (chromosomes align at equator), Anaphase (chromatids separate), Telophase (nuclei reform, chromosomes decondense). Followed by cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division). Results in diploid cells (2n chromosomes).
  • • Meiosis: Two divisions producing four genetically diverse gametes with half the chromosome number. Meiosis I: Homologous chromosomes pair and separate (reduces chromosome number). Meiosis II: Sister chromatids separate (similar to mitosis). Includes crossing over (genetic recombination) and independent assortment, creating genetic diversity. Results in haploid cells (n chromosomes).

Molecular Biology: DNA, RNA, and Protein Synthesis

Molecular biology examines biological processes at the molecular level, focusing on how DNA, RNA, and proteins interact to carry out cellular functions.

DNA Structure and Function:

  • • DNA Double Helix: Discovered by Watson and Crick (1953). Consists of two antiparallel strands wound around each other. Each strand has a sugar-phosphate backbone with nitrogenous bases (A, T, G, C) extending inward. Bases pair via hydrogen bonds: A-T (2 bonds), G-C (3 bonds). The sequence of bases encodes genetic information.
  • • Nucleotide Structure: Each nucleotide consists of a phosphate group, deoxyribose sugar, and nitrogenous base. Four bases: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C). The sequence of nucleotides forms the genetic code.
  • • DNA Replication: Semiconservative process where each strand serves as a template for a new complementary strand. Steps: Unwinding (helicase), Primer synthesis (primase), DNA synthesis (DNA polymerase adds nucleotides 5'→3'), Proofreading and repair (DNA polymerase corrects errors), Ligation (DNA ligase seals fragments). Results in two identical DNA molecules, each with one original and one new strand.
  • • Chromosomes: DNA wrapped around histone proteins to form chromatin. Chromatin condenses into chromosomes during cell division. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs): 22 autosome pairs and 1 sex chromosome pair (XX in females, XY in males).

The Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein

The central dogma describes the flow of genetic information: DNA is transcribed into RNA, which is translated into proteins.

Transcription: DNA to RNA

  • • RNA Polymerase: Enzyme that synthesizes RNA from DNA template. Binds to promoter region to initiate transcription.
  • • Process: Initiation (RNA polymerase binds promoter), Elongation (RNA synthesized 5'→3'), Termination (transcription ends, RNA released). RNA contains U (uracil) instead of T (thymine), pairing A-U instead of A-T.
  • • RNA Processing: Primary transcript (pre-mRNA) modified before leaving nucleus: 5' cap added, 3' poly-A tail added, introns removed (splicing), exons joined. Alternative splicing allows one gene to produce multiple protein variants.

Translation: RNA to Protein

  • • Genetic Code: Three-nucleotide codons specify amino acids. 64 possible codons: 61 code for amino acids, 3 are stop codons. Code is universal (same in nearly all organisms), redundant (multiple codons for same amino acid), and unambiguous (each codon specifies one amino acid).
  • • Transfer RNA (tRNA): Molecules that carry amino acids to ribosomes. Each tRNA has an anticodon (complementary to mRNA codon) and binds specific amino acid. Wobble hypothesis explains how some tRNAs recognize multiple codons.
  • • Ribosomal RNA (rRNA): Forms ribosome structure and catalyzes peptide bond formation. Ribosomes consist of large and small subunits, each containing rRNA and proteins.
  • • Translation Process: Initiation (ribosome binds mRNA, first tRNA with amino acid binds start codon AUG), Elongation (amino acids added one by one, peptide bonds formed), Termination (stop codon reached, protein released, ribosome dissociates).
  • • Protein Folding: Polypeptide chains fold into functional 3D structures. Primary structure (amino acid sequence), Secondary structure (alpha helices, beta sheets), Tertiary structure (overall 3D shape), Quaternary structure (multiple polypeptide chains). Chaperone proteins assist folding.

Gene Expression Regulation:

Gene expression is tightly controlled at multiple levels to ensure proteins are produced in correct amounts, at appropriate times, and in proper locations.

  • • Transcriptional Control: Most important level. Regulatory proteins (transcription factors) bind DNA sequences (promoters, enhancers, silencers) to activate or repress transcription. Epigenetic modifications (DNA methylation, histone modifications) also regulate gene expression without changing DNA sequence.
  • • Post-Transcriptional Control: Alternative splicing, mRNA stability (controlled by microRNAs and other factors), and mRNA localization affect which proteins are produced and where.
  • • Translational Control: Regulation of when and how often mRNA is translated. Initiation factors, regulatory proteins, and RNA interference (RNAi) control translation rates.
  • • Post-Translational Control: Protein modifications (phosphorylation, glycosylation, ubiquitination), protein degradation (proteasomes), and protein localization regulate protein activity and lifespan.

Genetics and Inheritance

Genetics examines how traits are inherited from parents to offspring and how genes interact to produce phenotypes (observable characteristics).

Mendelian Genetics:

  • • Mendel's Laws: Law of Segregation (alleles separate during gamete formation), Law of Independent Assortment (alleles for different traits assort independently), Law of Dominance (dominant alleles mask recessive alleles in heterozygous individuals).
  • • Genotype vs. Phenotype: Genotype is the genetic makeup (e.g., AA, Aa, aa). Phenotype is the observable expression (e.g., tall, short). Dominant alleles (capital letters) are expressed in heterozygotes; recessive alleles (lowercase) only expressed in homozygotes.
  • • Punnett Squares: Diagrams predicting offspring genotypes and phenotypes from parental genotypes. Used to calculate probabilities of inheritance patterns.

Complex Inheritance Patterns:

  • • Incomplete Dominance: Heterozygotes show intermediate phenotype (e.g., red × white flowers → pink offspring).
  • • Codominance: Both alleles expressed equally (e.g., AB blood type expressing both A and B antigens).
  • • Multiple Alleles: More than two alleles exist for a gene (e.g., ABO blood types: Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i).
  • • Polygenic Inheritance: Multiple genes contribute to one trait (e.g., height, skin color), producing continuous variation.
  • • Epistasis: One gene masks or modifies expression of another gene (e.g., albinism gene masking other pigmentation genes).
  • • Linked Genes: Genes located on same chromosome tend to be inherited together. Crossing over creates recombinant chromosomes, breaking linkage.
  • • Sex-Linked Inheritance: Genes on X or Y chromosomes show different inheritance patterns. X-linked traits (e.g., color blindness, hemophilia) more common in males (who have one X chromosome).

Mutations and Genetic Variation:

  • • Point Mutations: Single base pair changes. Types: Silent (no amino acid change due to redundancy), Missense (amino acid change), Nonsense (creates stop codon, truncating protein), Frameshift (insertion/deletion shifts reading frame, dramatically altering protein).
  • • Chromosomal Mutations: Changes in chromosome structure or number. Deletions, duplications, inversions, translocations affect gene expression. Aneuploidy (wrong chromosome number, e.g., Down syndrome: trisomy 21) and polyploidy (multiple chromosome sets) can have major effects.
  • • Genetic Variation Sources: Mutation (ultimate source), sexual reproduction (recombination, independent assortment), gene flow (migration introduces new alleles).

Evolution and Natural Selection

Evolution is the process by which species change over time through natural selection, genetic drift, mutation, and gene flow. Natural selection occurs when heritable traits that increase survival and reproduction become more common in populations over generations.

Evidence for Evolution:

  • • Fossil Record: Transitional forms show gradual changes over time. Stratigraphy reveals sequence of species appearance. Extinction events demonstrate species changes.
  • • Comparative Anatomy: Homologous structures (similar structure, different function, indicating common ancestry, e.g., forelimbs of mammals), Analogous structures (similar function, different structure, indicating convergent evolution, e.g., wings of birds and insects), Vestigial structures (reduced or non-functional remnants of ancestral features, e.g., human tailbone).
  • • Molecular Evidence: DNA and protein sequence comparisons reveal evolutionary relationships. More similar sequences indicate closer relatedness. Universal genetic code supports common ancestry.
  • • Embryological Evidence: Similar embryonic development patterns suggest common ancestry. Early embryos of vertebrates are remarkably similar, diverging later in development.
  • • Biogeography: Geographic distribution of species supports evolution. Related species found in nearby regions, islands show adaptive radiation from mainland ancestors.

Mechanisms of Evolution:

  • • Natural Selection: Differential survival and reproduction based on heritable traits. Four conditions: Variation exists, variation is heritable, more offspring produced than survive, survival/reproduction differential based on traits. Types: Directional (shifts trait distribution), Stabilizing (favors intermediate phenotypes), Disruptive (favors extremes), Sexual selection (selection based on mate preferences).
  • • Genetic Drift: Random changes in allele frequencies, especially in small populations. Causes: Bottleneck effect (population dramatically reduced, surviving population may have different allele frequencies), Founder effect (small group establishes new population with different allele frequencies). More significant in small populations, can lead to loss of genetic variation.
  • • Mutation: Source of genetic variation, creating new alleles. Most mutations are neutral or harmful; rare beneficial mutations can spread through population via natural selection. Mutation rate varies by organism and gene.
  • • Gene Flow: Movement of alleles between populations through migration. Introduces new alleles, reduces differences between populations. Can prevent speciation or cause rapid evolution if migrants carry beneficial alleles.
  • • Non-Random Mating: Selection of mates based on phenotypes. Inbreeding (mating between relatives) increases homozygosity and expression of recessive traits. Assortative mating (mating with similar individuals) can maintain genetic differences.

Speciation:

Speciation is the process by which new species arise. Species are groups of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring (biological species concept).

  • • Allopatric Speciation: Geographic isolation leads to speciation. Populations separated by barriers (mountains, water, distance) diverge genetically. Reproductive isolation develops through genetic changes.
  • • Sympatric Speciation: Speciation without geographic isolation. Can occur through polyploidy (especially in plants), habitat differentiation, or sexual selection. Less common than allopatric speciation.
  • • Reproductive Isolation: Barriers preventing interbreeding. Prezygotic barriers (before fertilization): Habitat isolation, temporal isolation (different mating times), behavioral isolation (different courtship), mechanical isolation (incompatible anatomy), gametic isolation (gametes don't fuse). Postzygotic barriers (after fertilization): Reduced hybrid viability, reduced hybrid fertility, hybrid breakdown.

Ecology: Interactions in Nature

Ecology studies interactions between organisms and their environment, from individual organisms to entire ecosystems and the biosphere. Understanding ecology is essential for conservation, environmental management, and understanding biodiversity.

Ecological Levels of Organization:

  1. 1.
    Organismal Ecology: Individual adaptations to environment. Examines how organisms' physiology, morphology, and behavior enable survival in specific habitats. Includes adaptations to temperature, water availability, light, nutrients, and other abiotic factors.
    Examples: Desert plants with thick cuticles, arctic animals with thick fur, deep-sea fish with bioluminescence
  2. 2.
    Population Ecology: Groups of same species in an area, studying growth, distribution, and dynamics. Examines birth rates, death rates, immigration, emigration, population growth models (exponential, logistic), carrying capacity, and population regulation.
    Growth equation: dN/dt = rN (exponential) or dN/dt = rN(1-N/K) (logistic), where N = population size, r = growth rate, K = carrying capacity
  3. 3.
    Community Ecology: Multiple species interacting in an area, including competition, predation, symbiosis, herbivory, and parasitism. Examines species diversity, community structure, trophic levels, food webs, and succession (changes in community composition over time).
    Interactions: Competition (resource limitation), Predation (predator-prey dynamics), Mutualism (both benefit), Commensalism (one benefits, other unaffected), Parasitism (one benefits, other harmed)
  4. 4.
    Ecosystem Ecology: Community plus abiotic factors (non-living components), studying energy flow and nutrient cycling. Examines primary productivity (rate of biomass production), energy pyramids (only ~10% energy transferred between trophic levels), biogeochemical cycles (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, water), and ecosystem services.
    Trophic levels: Producers (autotrophs), Primary consumers (herbivores), Secondary consumers (carnivores), Tertiary consumers (top predators), Decomposers (detritivores)
  5. 5. Biosphere: Global sum of all ecosystems. Examines global patterns of biodiversity, climate zones, biomes (major ecosystems with characteristic climate and organisms), and human impacts on global systems.

Energy Flow and Nutrient Cycling:

  • • Energy Flow: Unidirectional (enters as sunlight, exits as heat). Producers convert solar energy to chemical energy through photosynthesis. Energy flows through food chains: Producers → Primary consumers → Secondary consumers → Tertiary consumers. Only ~10% energy transferred between levels (10% rule), rest lost as heat/metabolism/waste. Limits food chain length (typically 4-5 levels).
  • • Nutrient Cycling: Biogeochemical cycles recycle nutrients. Carbon cycle: Photosynthesis removes CO₂, respiration releases CO₂, decomposition returns carbon to soil. Nitrogen cycle: Nitrogen fixation (N₂ → NH₃ by bacteria), nitrification (NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻), denitrification (NO₃⁻ → N₂). Phosphorus cycle: Weathering releases phosphate, absorbed by plants, cycles through food web, returned via decomposition.

Biodiversity and Conservation:

  • • Biodiversity: Variety of life at all levels: genetic diversity (variation within species), species diversity (number and relative abundance of species), ecosystem diversity (variety of ecosystems). High biodiversity increases ecosystem stability, productivity, and resilience.
  • • Threats to Biodiversity: Habitat destruction (deforestation, urbanization), climate change, pollution, overexploitation (hunting, fishing, poaching), invasive species (displace native species), disease. Human activities are primary cause of current biodiversity loss.
  • • Conservation Strategies: Protected areas (national parks, reserves), habitat restoration, species reintroduction, captive breeding programs, sustainable resource use, international cooperation. Conservation biology applies ecological principles to preserve biodiversity.

Physiology and Homeostasis

Physiology examines how organisms function, maintaining homeostasis through coordinated systems. Homeostasis is the maintenance of stable internal conditions despite external changes, essential for survival.

Homeostatic Mechanisms:

  • • Negative Feedback: Primary mechanism maintaining homeostasis. Response opposes stimulus, returning system to set point. Example: Body temperature regulation (sweating when hot, shivering when cold), blood glucose regulation (insulin lowers glucose, glucagon raises glucose), blood pH regulation.
  • • Positive Feedback: Response amplifies stimulus, moving system away from set point. Less common, used for processes needing rapid completion. Example: Blood clotting cascade, childbirth (oxytocin contractions), action potential generation (sodium channels opening).
  • • Feedback Loop Components: Sensor (detects change), Control center (compares to set point), Effector (produces response). Nervous and endocrine systems coordinate these components.

Major Organ Systems:

  1. 1.
    Nervous System: Rapid communication through electrical and chemical signals. Central nervous system (brain, spinal cord) processes information. Peripheral nervous system (nerves) transmits signals. Neurons transmit electrical signals (action potentials) and chemical signals (neurotransmitters) across synapses. Controls voluntary and involuntary functions, coordinates responses, integrates sensory information.
    Major divisions: Somatic (voluntary control), Autonomic (involuntary: sympathetic "fight/flight", parasympathetic "rest/digest")
  2. 2.
    Endocrine System: Slower, longer-lasting regulation through hormones (chemical messengers). Glands secrete hormones into bloodstream, targeting specific cells with receptors. Coordinates growth, development, metabolism, reproduction, stress response. Examples: Insulin (glucose regulation), Growth hormone (growth), Thyroid hormones (metabolism), Adrenaline (stress response), Sex hormones (reproduction).
    Major glands: Pituitary (master gland), Thyroid, Adrenal, Pancreas, Gonads (testes/ovaries)
  3. 3.
    Circulatory System: Transports nutrients, gases, waste products, hormones, and immune cells throughout body. Heart pumps blood through vessels (arteries, veins, capillaries). Blood components: Red blood cells (carry oxygen via hemoglobin), White blood cells (immune function), Platelets (clotting), Plasma (liquid component with proteins, nutrients, waste). Closed circulatory system in vertebrates.
    Cardiac cycle: Systole (contraction), Diastole (relaxation). Blood flow: Heart → Arteries → Capillaries (exchange) → Veins → Heart
  4. 4.
    Respiratory System: Gas exchange (oxygen in, carbon dioxide out). Breathing (ventilation) moves air in/out of lungs. External respiration: Exchange between air and blood in alveoli (diffusion across thin membranes). Internal respiration: Exchange between blood and body tissues. Oxygen transported by hemoglobin in red blood cells; CO₂ transported as bicarbonate ions, dissolved gas, or bound to hemoglobin.
    Process: Inspiration (diaphragm contracts, volume increases, pressure decreases, air enters) → Exchange → Expiration (diaphragm relaxes, volume decreases, pressure increases, air exits)
  5. 5.
    Digestive System: Breaks down food and absorbs nutrients. Mechanical digestion (chewing, churning) and chemical digestion (enzymes). Organs: Mouth (salivary amylase starts carbohydrate digestion), Stomach (pepsin digests proteins, HCl activates enzymes), Small intestine (most digestion and absorption: carbohydrates → glucose, proteins → amino acids, fats → fatty acids/glycerol), Large intestine (water absorption, waste formation). Accessory organs: Liver (bile production, detoxification), Pancreas (digestive enzymes, bicarbonate), Gallbladder (bile storage).
    Nutrient absorption: Villi and microvilli increase surface area in small intestine. Active transport, facilitated diffusion, simple diffusion move nutrients into blood/lymph
  6. 6. Excretory System: Removes waste products and maintains water/salt balance. Kidneys filter blood, producing urine. Nephron functional unit: Glomerulus (filtration), Tubules (reabsorption of water/nutrients, secretion of wastes). Urine concentration regulated by hormones (ADH, aldosterone). Also includes lungs (CO₂ removal), skin (sweat), liver (urea production).
  7. 7. Immune System: Defends against pathogens and disease. Innate immunity (non-specific: skin, mucus, macrophages, inflammation, fever) provides immediate defense. Adaptive immunity (specific: B cells produce antibodies, T cells kill infected cells, memory cells provide long-term protection) develops after exposure. Vaccination stimulates adaptive immunity without disease.
  8. 8. Musculoskeletal System: Provides structure, movement, and protection. Skeletal system: Bones (structure, protection, mineral storage, blood cell production), Joints (allow movement), Ligaments (connect bones). Muscular system: Skeletal muscles (voluntary movement), Cardiac muscle (heart contraction), Smooth muscle (involuntary, organs/blood vessels). Muscle contraction: Actin and myosin filaments slide past each other (sliding filament model), requiring ATP and calcium.

Metabolism and Energy Production

Metabolism encompasses all chemical reactions in cells, including energy production (catabolism) and biosynthesis (anabolism). Understanding metabolism is essential for understanding how organisms obtain and use energy.

Cellular Respiration:

Process converting glucose and other fuels into ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the energy currency of cells. Occurs in multiple stages:

  1. 1.
    Glycolysis: Occurs in cytoplasm. Glucose (6C) → 2 Pyruvate (3C each) + 2 ATP + 2 NADH. Anaerobic process (no oxygen required). Investment phase (uses 2 ATP) and payoff phase (produces 4 ATP, net 2 ATP).
  2. 2.
    Pyruvate Oxidation: In mitochondria. Pyruvate → Acetyl CoA + CO₂ + NADH. Links glycolysis to Krebs cycle.
  3. 3.
    Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle): In mitochondrial matrix. Acetyl CoA enters cycle, CO₂ released, produces ATP, NADH, FADH₂. For each glucose: 2 turns of cycle, produces 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂.
  4. 4.
    Electron Transport Chain (ETC) and Oxidative Phosphorylation: In inner mitochondrial membrane. NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons, electrons pass through protein complexes, energy pumps H⁺ across membrane creating gradient, H⁺ flows back through ATP synthase, ATP synthesized. Oxygen is final electron acceptor, forming H₂O. Produces ~28-34 ATP per glucose. Total: ~30-38 ATP per glucose.

Photosynthesis:

Process converting light energy to chemical energy (glucose). Occurs in chloroplasts of plants, algae, and some bacteria. Overall equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

  • • Light-Dependent Reactions: In thylakoid membranes. Light absorbed by chlorophyll, electrons excited, electrons flow through ETC, H⁺ pumped into thylakoid space, ATP and NADPH produced (chemiosmosis), H₂O split (photolysis), O₂ released. Photosystems I and II work together.
  • • Light-Independent Reactions (Calvin Cycle): In stroma. Uses ATP and NADPH from light reactions. Carbon fixation (CO₂ + RuBP → 3PG), Reduction (3PG → G3P using ATP and NADPH), Regeneration (RuBP regenerated using ATP). For each turn: 3 CO₂ → 1 G3P (3-carbon sugar). 6 turns produce 1 glucose. C₃ photosynthesis uses Calvin cycle directly.
  • • C₄ and CAM Photosynthesis: Adaptations reducing photorespiration. C₄ plants (corn, sugarcane) fix CO₂ into 4-carbon compounds, concentrating CO₂. CAM plants (cacti) open stomata at night, fixing CO₂, then use it during day. Both more efficient in hot, dry conditions.
