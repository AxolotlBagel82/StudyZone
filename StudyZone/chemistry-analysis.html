Chemistry: In Depth Analysis

Comprehensive exploration of matter and its transformations

Introduction to Chemistry

Chemistry is the science that studies matter, its properties, composition, structure, and the changes it undergoes during chemical reactions. Chemistry connects the microscopic world of atoms and molecules to the macroscopic world we observe, explaining everything from why water freezes to how our bodies convert food into energy.

The field encompasses multiple branches: organic chemistry (carbon compounds), inorganic chemistry (non-carbon compounds), physical chemistry (energy and dynamics), analytical chemistry (identification and measurement), and biochemistry (chemistry of living systems). Understanding chemistry is fundamental to fields including medicine, engineering, environmental science, and materials science.

Chemistry's impact on society is profound: pharmaceuticals combat disease, fertilizers feed populations, materials science creates new technologies, and environmental chemistry addresses pollution and sustainability challenges. The principles of chemistry underlie all biological processes and most physical phenomena.

Atomic Structure: The Foundation of Matter

Atoms are the fundamental building blocks of matter. Understanding atomic structure is essential for explaining chemical behavior, bonding, and reactions. Modern atomic theory describes atoms as consisting of a dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by a cloud of negatively charged electrons.

Atomic Components:

  • • Protons: Positively charged particles in the nucleus. Mass ≈ 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg (1 atomic mass unit, amu). Number of protons defines the element (atomic number, Z). Each element has unique number of protons.
  • • Neutrons: Neutral particles in the nucleus. Mass ≈ 1 amu (slightly heavier than protons). Number of neutrons can vary in same element (isotopes). Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons.
  • • Electrons: Negatively charged particles orbiting nucleus. Mass ≈ 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg (1/1836 amu, negligible). In neutral atoms, number of electrons = number of protons. Electrons determine chemical properties and bonding behavior.
  • • Isotopes: Atoms of same element with different numbers of neutrons (different mass numbers). Same chemical properties but different masses. Examples: Carbon-12 (6p, 6n), Carbon-13 (6p, 7n), Carbon-14 (6p, 8n, radioactive).

Electron Configuration and Quantum Mechanics:

Electrons occupy regions of space called orbitals, described by quantum mechanics. The arrangement of electrons determines chemical properties.

  • • Quantum Numbers: Four numbers describe each electron's location and properties: Principal quantum number (n, energy level: 1, 2, 3...), Azimuthal quantum number (l, orbital shape: 0=s, 1=p, 2=d, 3=f), Magnetic quantum number (m_l, orbital orientation), Spin quantum number (m_s, electron spin: +½ or -½).
  • • Orbitals: Regions of space where electrons are most likely to be found. s orbitals (spherical, 1 per energy level), p orbitals (dumbbell-shaped, 3 per energy level, oriented along x, y, z axes), d orbitals (complex shapes, 5 per energy level), f orbitals (very complex, 7 per energy level). Each orbital holds maximum 2 electrons (Pauli exclusion principle).
  • • Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first. Order: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p...
  • • Hund's Rule: When filling degenerate orbitals (same energy), electrons occupy separate orbitals with parallel spins before pairing. Maximizes unpaired electrons.
  • • Valence Electrons: Electrons in outermost energy level (highest n). Determine chemical reactivity and bonding. Elements in same group have same number of valence electrons, explaining periodic trends.

Periodic Table and Periodic Trends:

The periodic table organizes elements by atomic number, revealing periodic trends in properties that reflect electron configurations.

  • • Periods: Horizontal rows. Number indicates highest energy level with electrons. Elements in same period have same number of electron shells.
  • • Groups/Families: Vertical columns. Elements in same group have same number of valence electrons and similar chemical properties. Examples: Group 1 (alkali metals: 1 valence electron), Group 17 (halogens: 7 valence electrons), Group 18 (noble gases: 8 valence electrons, stable).
  • • Atomic Radius: Size of atom. Decreases left to right across period (increased nuclear charge pulls electrons closer), increases top to bottom down group (additional electron shells).
  • • Ionization Energy: Energy required to remove an electron. Increases left to right (stronger attraction to nucleus), decreases top to bottom (electrons farther from nucleus). First ionization energy: removing first electron. Successive ionization energies higher (removing electron from positive ion harder).
  • • Electronegativity: Ability to attract electrons in a bond. Increases left to right, decreases top to bottom. Fluorine (most electronegative, 4.0) → Cesium/Francium (least electronegative, ~0.7). Differences in electronegativity determine bond polarity.
  • • Electron Affinity: Energy change when atom gains electron. More negative = stronger attraction. Generally increases left to right (except noble gases), varies down groups.

Chemical Bonding: How Atoms Connect

Chemical bonds form when atoms interact, resulting in stable structures with lower energy than separate atoms. The type of bond depends on the atoms involved and their electron configurations.

Ionic Bonds:

  • • Formation: Complete transfer of electrons from metal (low electronegativity) to nonmetal (high electronegativity). Metal loses electrons → becomes positive ion (cation). Nonmetal gains electrons → becomes negative ion (anion). Oppositely charged ions attract electrostatically.
  • • Characteristics: Strong bonds, high melting and boiling points, form crystalline solids, conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions free to move), typically soluble in polar solvents like water. Example: NaCl (sodium chloride, table salt): Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions in crystal lattice.
  • • Ionic Compounds: Ratio of ions determined by charges (to achieve neutrality). Formula shows smallest whole number ratio (empirical formula). Example: CaCl₂ (calcium loses 2 electrons, each chlorine gains 1 electron, need 2 chlorines per calcium).
  • • Lattice Energy: Energy released when gaseous ions form solid crystal. Higher charges and smaller ions → greater lattice energy → stronger bonds → higher melting points.

Covalent Bonds:

  • • Formation: Sharing of electron pairs between atoms, typically between nonmetals. Each atom contributes one electron to shared pair. Achieves stable electron configuration (usually octet rule: 8 valence electrons) without transferring electrons.
  • • Single, Double, Triple Bonds: Single bond (1 shared pair, σ bond), Double bond (2 shared pairs: 1 σ + 1 π bond), Triple bond (3 shared pairs: 1 σ + 2 π bonds). More bonds = shorter bond length, stronger bond, higher bond energy.
  • • Polar vs. Nonpolar Covalent: Nonpolar: Equal sharing (same electronegativity, e.g., H₂, O₂, C-H). Polar: Unequal sharing (different electronegativity, e.g., H₂O, HCl). Creates partial charges: δ+ on less electronegative atom, δ- on more electronegative atom. Dipole moment: measure of polarity.
  • • Lewis Structures: Diagrams showing valence electrons and bonds. Rules: Count valence electrons, connect atoms with single bonds, complete octets (except H with 2), place extra electrons on most electronegative atom, use double/triple bonds if needed. Resonance: Multiple valid structures for same molecule (actual structure is average/hybrid).
  • • VSEPR Theory (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion): Electron pairs (bonding and lone pairs) repel each other, arranging to maximize distance. Predicts molecular geometry. Shapes: Linear (2 electron domains), Trigonal planar (3), Tetrahedral (4), Trigonal bipyramidal (5), Octahedral (6). Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs, distorting geometry.
  • • Hybridization: Mixing of atomic orbitals to form hybrid orbitals for bonding. sp (linear, 2 electron domains), sp² (trigonal planar, 3 domains), sp³ (tetrahedral, 4 domains), sp³d (trigonal bipyramidal, 5), sp³d² (octahedral, 6). Explains observed bond angles and molecular shapes.

Metallic Bonds:

  • • Formation: Delocalized valence electrons shared among all metal atoms. Metal atoms lose valence electrons easily (low ionization energy), creating "sea of electrons" surrounding positive metal ions.
  • • Properties: High electrical conductivity (free electrons carry current), high thermal conductivity (electrons transfer heat), malleability (can be hammered into sheets), ductility (can be drawn into wires), luster (electrons reflect light), typically high melting points (except mercury, group 12).
  • • Alloys: Mixtures of metals (or metal + nonmetal). Often superior properties to pure metals: Steel (iron + carbon) stronger, Bronze (copper + tin) harder, Brass (copper + zinc) more corrosion-resistant. Interstitial alloys (smaller atoms fit in spaces) and substitutional alloys (similar-sized atoms replace each other).

Intermolecular Forces (Van der Waals Forces):

Weaker than chemical bonds but important for determining physical properties like melting/boiling points, solubility, and phase transitions.

  • • Hydrogen Bonds: Strong dipole-dipole interaction. Hydrogen bonded to highly electronegative atom (N, O, F) attracted to lone pair on another electronegative atom. 5-10% strength of covalent bond but much stronger than other intermolecular forces. Crucial for: Water's high boiling point, DNA double helix structure, protein secondary structure (α-helix, β-sheet), ice density (less dense than liquid water, floats).
  • • Dipole-Dipole Forces: Attractions between polar molecules. Positive end of one molecule attracts negative end of another. Stronger than London forces, weaker than hydrogen bonds. Example: HCl, HBr.
  • • London Dispersion Forces (Van der Waals Forces): Weakest intermolecular forces. Temporary dipoles created by electron movement induce dipoles in neighboring molecules. Present in all molecules, but especially important for nonpolar molecules (only intermolecular force they have). Strength increases with molecular size (more electrons = stronger temporary dipoles). Example: Noble gases, hydrocarbons like methane, propane.
  • • Influence on Properties: Stronger intermolecular forces → Higher melting/boiling points (more energy needed to overcome), Higher viscosity (resistance to flow), Higher surface tension (cohesive forces at surface). Hydrogen bonding > Dipole-dipole > London forces.

Chemical Reactions: Transformations of Matter

Chemical reactions involve breaking and forming bonds, resulting in the transformation of reactants into products. Understanding reaction types, mechanisms, and conditions is fundamental to chemistry.

Balancing Chemical Equations:

Chemical equations must satisfy the law of conservation of mass: atoms are neither created nor destroyed. Coefficients are adjusted to balance atoms on both sides.

  • • Stoichiometry: Quantitative relationships in chemical reactions. Coefficients represent mole ratios. Used to calculate amounts of reactants needed or products formed. Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O means 2 moles H₂ react with 1 mole O₂ to produce 2 moles H₂O.
  • • Limiting Reactant: Reactant that runs out first, limiting product amount. Excess reactant remains unreacted. Calculations identify limiting reactant and theoretical yield (maximum product possible).
  • • Percent Yield: Actual yield / Theoretical yield × 100%. Always less than 100% due to incomplete reactions, side reactions, losses. Used to evaluate reaction efficiency.

Types of Chemical Reactions:

  1. 1.
    Synthesis (Combination): Combination of reactants to form products (A + B → AB). Two or more substances combine to form more complex product. Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (hydrogen and oxygen form water), N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ (nitrogen and hydrogen form ammonia).
  2. 2.
    Decomposition: Breaking down compounds into simpler substances (AB → A + B). One compound splits into two or more simpler products. Often requires energy (heat, electricity, light). Example: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ (hydrogen peroxide decomposes), CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (calcium carbonate decomposes when heated).
  3. 3.
    Single Replacement (Displacement): One element replaces another in a compound (A + BC → AC + B). More reactive element replaces less reactive element. Activity series predicts if reaction occurs: Elements above H replace H from acids; elements above others replace them from compounds. Example: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂ (zinc replaces hydrogen), Cu + 2AgNO₃ → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag (copper replaces silver).
  4. 4.
    Double Replacement (Exchange): Exchange of ions between compounds (AB + CD → AD + CB). Ions "swap partners". Occurs if precipitate forms, gas produced, or weak electrolyte formed. Example: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃ (silver chloride precipitate), HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O (acid-base neutralization).
  5. 5.
    Combustion: Rapid reaction with oxygen, producing heat and light. Hydrocarbons + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O. Complete combustion produces CO₂ and H₂O; incomplete combustion produces CO and/or C (soot). Example: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O (methane combustion), C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O (propane combustion). Essential for energy production.
  6. 6.
    Redox (Oxidation-Reduction) Reactions: Involve electron transfer. Oxidation: Loss of electrons (increase in oxidation number). Reduction: Gain of electrons (decrease in oxidation number). Redox reactions always involve both: oxidizing agent gains electrons (reduced), reducing agent loses electrons (oxidized). Fundamental in: Cellular respiration (glucose oxidized to CO₂), Photosynthesis (CO₂ reduced to glucose), Corrosion (iron oxidized), Batteries (electrons flow from anode to cathode).
  7. 7.
    Acid-Base Reactions (Neutralization): Acid + Base → Salt + Water. Arrhenius definition: Acids produce H⁺, bases produce OH⁻. Bronsted-Lowry definition: Acids donate H⁺ (protons), bases accept H⁺. Example: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O. pH measures acidity: pH < 7 (acidic), pH = 7 (neutral), pH > 7 (basic). Buffer solutions resist pH changes.

Reaction Mechanisms:

  • • Elementary Steps: Individual steps in reaction mechanism. Molecularity: Number of molecules participating (unimolecular: 1, bimolecular: 2, termolecular: 3, rare). Rate-determining step: Slowest step, determines overall rate.
  • • Catalysts: Substances that increase reaction rate without being consumed. Lower activation energy, providing alternative pathway. Homogeneous catalysts (same phase as reactants) and heterogeneous catalysts (different phase, e.g., solid catalyst with gaseous reactants). Enzymes are biological catalysts (proteins that catalyze biochemical reactions).
  • • Intermediates: Species formed and consumed during reaction, not in final products. Reactants → Intermediate → Products. Often unstable, difficult to isolate.

Reaction Kinetics: Rates of Chemical Reactions

Chemical kinetics studies reaction rates (how fast reactions occur) and mechanisms (how reactions proceed step-by-step). Understanding kinetics is essential for controlling reactions and understanding chemical processes.

Reaction Rate:

  • • Definition: Change in concentration of reactant or product per unit time. Rate = -Δ[Reactant]/Δt = +Δ[Product]/Δt. Units: mol/L·s or M/s. Rate decreases as reaction proceeds (reactants consumed).
  • • Average vs. Instantaneous Rate: Average rate: Change over time interval. Instantaneous rate: Rate at specific moment (slope of tangent on concentration vs. time graph). Initial rate: Rate at start of reaction (t = 0), used to determine rate law.

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate:

  1. 1.
    Concentration: Higher concentration → More frequent collisions → Faster rate. Collision theory: Reactions require collisions with sufficient energy (activation energy) and proper orientation. Rate law: Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n, where k = rate constant, m and n = reaction orders (determined experimentally), sum = overall order.
  2. 2.
    Temperature: Higher temperature → Higher kinetic energy → More collisions with sufficient energy (above activation energy) → Faster rate. Arrhenius equation: k = Ae^(-Ea/RT), where A = frequency factor, Ea = activation energy, R = gas constant, T = temperature. Rule of thumb: 10°C increase approximately doubles rate (for many reactions).
  3. 3.
    Surface Area (for heterogeneous reactions): Larger surface area → More collision sites → Faster rate. Example: Powdered zinc reacts faster than zinc chunk with acid. Important in reactions involving solids.
  4. 4.
    Catalysts: Lower activation energy → More molecules have sufficient energy → Faster rate. Catalysts provide alternative pathway with lower energy barrier. Not consumed in reaction, can be recovered. Types: Enzymes (biological), transition metals (industrial processes like Haber process for ammonia), acid/base catalysts.
  5. 5. Nature of Reactants: Strong bonds require more energy to break → Slower rate. Ionic compounds often react faster than covalent compounds. Gases react faster than liquids, which react faster than solids (more mobile particles).

Reaction Orders:

  • • Zero Order: Rate independent of reactant concentration. Rate = k. Concentration decreases linearly with time. Rare, often when reactant is in excess or catalyst is saturated.
  • • First Order: Rate proportional to one reactant concentration. Rate = k[A]. Concentration decreases exponentially: ln[A] vs. t is linear (slope = -k). Half-life (t₁/₂): Time for half of reactant to decompose, constant for first-order reactions: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k. Examples: Radioactive decay, many decomposition reactions.
  • • Second Order: Rate proportional to square of concentration or product of two concentrations. Rate = k[A]² or k[A][B]. Example: 2NO₂ → 2NO + O₂. Half-life increases as concentration decreases.

Thermodynamics: Energy in Chemical Processes

Thermodynamics describes energy changes in chemical processes, predicting whether reactions occur spontaneously and how energy flows. Understanding thermodynamics is essential for predicting reaction feasibility and understanding energy transformations.

First Law of Thermodynamics (Conservation of Energy):

  • • Energy Conservation: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted between forms. ΔE = q + w, where ΔE = change in internal energy, q = heat, w = work. In chemical reactions, energy changes manifest as heat and/or work.
  • • Enthalpy (H): Heat content at constant pressure. ΔH = H_products - H_reactants. Enthalpy change (ΔH) equals heat absorbed or released at constant pressure. Negative ΔH (exothermic): Releases heat, products have less energy than reactants. Positive ΔH (endothermic): Absorbs heat, products have more energy than reactants. Measured in kJ/mol.
  • • Hess's Law: Overall enthalpy change equals sum of enthalpy changes for individual steps. Allows calculation of ΔH from known reactions. Independent of pathway: Only initial and final states matter. Used to determine ΔH for reactions difficult to measure directly.
  • • Standard Enthalpy of Formation (ΔH°f): Enthalpy change when 1 mole of compound forms from elements in standard states. ΔH°f = 0 for elements in standard states. Used to calculate ΔH° for reactions: ΔH° = ΣnΔH°f(products) - ΣmΔH°f(reactants).
  • • Bond Energies: Energy required to break bonds (endothermic, positive) or released when bonds form (exothermic, negative). ΔH ≈ Σ(Bond energies broken) - Σ(Bond energies formed). Average bond energies used (actual values vary with molecular environment).

Second Law of Thermodynamics and Entropy:

  • • Entropy (S): Measure of disorder or randomness. More disordered = higher entropy. Units: J/mol·K. Absolute entropy can be determined (third law: S = 0 at absolute zero for perfect crystal).
  • • Second Law: Total entropy of universe increases in spontaneous processes. Isolated systems tend toward maximum entropy (maximum disorder). ΔS_universe = ΔS_system + ΔS_surroundings > 0 for spontaneous processes.
  • • Entropy Changes: Increase entropy: Phase transitions (solid → liquid → gas), temperature increase, volume increase (gases), number of particles increase, mixing, dissolution. Decrease entropy: Condensation, freezing, complex formation. Gas > Liquid > Solid (entropy order).
  • • Standard Entropy (S°): Entropy at standard conditions. ΔS° = ΣnS°(products) - ΣmS°(reactants). Can predict entropy changes from standard values.

Gibbs Free Energy and Spontaneity:

  • • Gibbs Free Energy (G): Combines enthalpy and entropy: G = H - TS. At constant temperature and pressure: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS. Predicts spontaneity and useful work available from reaction.
  • • Spontaneity Criteria: Negative ΔG (ΔG < 0): Spontaneous reaction, proceeds forward. Positive ΔG (ΔG > 0): Non-spontaneous, reverse reaction spontaneous. Zero ΔG (ΔG = 0): At equilibrium, no net change. ΔG° = ΔH° - TΔS° (standard conditions).
  • • Temperature Effects: Temperature influences spontaneity through -TΔS term. Endothermic reactions (ΔH > 0) can be spontaneous if ΔS > 0 (entropy increase drives reaction). At high temperature: -TΔS dominates (entropy-driven). At low temperature: ΔH dominates (enthalpy-driven).
  • • Free Energy and Equilibrium: ΔG° = -RT ln K, where R = gas constant, T = temperature, K = equilibrium constant. Large negative ΔG° → Large K → Products favored. Large positive ΔG° → Small K → Reactants favored. At equilibrium: ΔG = 0, Q = K.

Chemical Equilibrium: Dynamic Balance

Chemical equilibrium occurs when forward and reverse reaction rates are equal, resulting in constant concentrations (though reactions continue). Understanding equilibrium is crucial for predicting reaction outcomes and optimizing conditions.

Equilibrium Concepts:

  • • Dynamic Equilibrium: Forward and reverse rates equal, concentrations constant, but reactions continue. Macroscopic properties constant, microscopic processes continue. Reversible reactions reach equilibrium: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD.
  • • Equilibrium Constant (K): Ratio of product and reactant concentrations at equilibrium. K = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b. Pure solids and liquids not included (activity = 1). K_c (concentrations) and K_p (partial pressures for gases). Large K (>1): Products favored at equilibrium. Small K (<1): Reactants favored at equilibrium.
  • • Reaction Quotient (Q): Same expression as K but using concentrations at any point. Q < K: Forward reaction favored (toward products). Q = K: At equilibrium. Q > K: Reverse reaction favored (toward reactants). Used to predict direction of reaction.
  • • Relationship Between K and ΔG°: ΔG° = -RT ln K. Negative ΔG° → K > 1 (products favored). Positive ΔG° → K < 1 (reactants favored). Allows calculation of equilibrium constant from thermodynamic data.

Le Chatelier's Principle:

When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to counteract the disturbance and re-establish equilibrium.

  • • Concentration Changes: Increase reactant concentration → Shift toward products (consume added reactant). Increase product concentration → Shift toward reactants (consume added product). Decrease reactant → Shift toward reactants. Decrease product → Shift toward products.
  • • Pressure Changes (for gases): Increase pressure → Shift toward fewer gas molecules (reduces pressure). Decrease pressure → Shift toward more gas molecules (increases pressure). No effect if equal numbers of gas molecules on both sides. Volume changes have opposite effect (decrease volume = increase pressure).
  • • Temperature Changes: Exothermic reaction (ΔH < 0): Increase temperature → Shift toward reactants (endothermic direction). Decrease temperature → Shift toward products (exothermic direction). Endothermic reaction (ΔH > 0): Increase temperature → Shift toward products. Decrease temperature → Shift toward reactants. Temperature changes alter K (equilibrium constant changes with temperature).
  • • Catalysts: No effect on equilibrium position or K. Catalysts speed up both forward and reverse reactions equally, so equilibrium reached faster but composition unchanged. Only affects rate, not thermodynamics.

Organic Chemistry: Carbon-Containing Compounds

Organic chemistry focuses on carbon-containing compounds. Carbon's unique ability to form four covalent bonds and catenate (form chains and rings) creates immense molecular diversity, forming the basis of life and many materials.

Carbon Bonding and Hybridization:

  • • Carbon's Versatility: Forms 4 covalent bonds (tetravalent), can form single, double, or triple bonds, can catenate (bond to itself forming chains), can form rings, bonds to many other elements (H, N, O, S, P, halogens). Millions of organic compounds possible.
  • • Hybridization in Carbon: sp³ (tetrahedral, 109.5°, single bonds, e.g., methane CH₄, ethane C₂H₆), sp² (trigonal planar, 120°, double bonds, e.g., ethene C₂H₄, benzene C₆H₆), sp (linear, 180°, triple bonds, e.g., ethyne C₂H₂). Explains bond angles and molecular geometries.

Hydrocarbons: Fundamental Organic Molecules

  1. 1.
    Alkanes: Saturated hydrocarbons (only single bonds). General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Names end in -ane. Examples: Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆), Propane (C₃H₈), Butane (C₄H₁₀). Straight-chain and branched isomers. Naming: IUPAC nomenclature (longest chain, number substituents, alphabetical order). Properties: Nonpolar, low boiling points (increase with size), used as fuels.
  2. 2.
    Alkenes: Unsaturated hydrocarbons with double bonds. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ. Names end in -ene. Examples: Ethene (C₂H₄), Propene (C₃H₆). Geometric isomers (cis/trans) possible when each double-bond carbon has different substituents. More reactive than alkanes (double bond can undergo addition reactions).
  3. 3.
    Alkynes: Unsaturated hydrocarbons with triple bonds. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂. Names end in -yne. Examples: Ethyne (C₂H₂, acetylene), Propyne (C₃H₄). Most reactive hydrocarbons (triple bond very reactive). Used in welding (acetylene torch).
  4. 4.
    Aromatic Compounds: Contain benzene ring (C₆H₆) or similar structures. Benzene has delocalized electrons (resonance), making it unusually stable. Substituted benzenes: Toluene (methylbenzene), Phenol (hydroxybenzene), Aniline (aminobenzene). Many have distinctive odors. Important in pharmaceuticals, dyes, plastics.

Functional Groups: Chemical Reactivity

Functional groups are specific arrangements of atoms that determine chemical properties and reactivity. Compounds with same functional group have similar properties.

  1. 1.
    Alcohols (-OH): Contain hydroxyl group. Naming: -ol suffix. Examples: Methanol (CH₃OH), Ethanol (C₂H₅OH, alcohol in beverages), Propanol (C₃H₇OH). Primary (1°): C-OH, Secondary (2°): C-C-OH, Tertiary (3°): C-C(OH)-C. Can hydrogen bond (high boiling points), soluble in water (small alcohols). Important in: Biological systems, solvents, fuels, pharmaceuticals.
  2. 2.
    Aldehydes (-CHO) and Ketones (-C=O): Carbonyl groups. Aldehydes: Terminal C=O (formaldehyde HCHO, acetaldehyde CH₃CHO). Ketones: C=O between carbons (acetone CH₃COCH₃). Naming: Aldehydes end in -al, ketones in -one. Polar, can hydrogen bond (not as strong as alcohols). Important in: Biological processes (sugars), solvents, pharmaceuticals.
  3. 3.
    Carboxylic Acids (-COOH): Contain carboxyl group. Naming: -oic acid suffix. Examples: Formic acid (HCOOH, ant venom), Acetic acid (CH₃COOH, vinegar), Stearic acid (fatty acid). Can hydrogen bond (dimers), acidic (donate H⁺), higher boiling points. Important in: Amino acids, fatty acids (lipids), carboxylic acid derivatives.
  4. 4.
    Esters (-COO-): Derivatives of carboxylic acids. Formed from acid + alcohol → ester + water. Naming: Alkyl from alcohol + -ate from acid. Examples: Ethyl acetate (fruit smells), fats and oils (triglycerides). Often pleasant odors (fruits, flowers). Important in: Fats, oils, waxes, perfumes, flavors, plastics (polyesters).
  5. 5.
    Amines (-NH₂, -NHR, -NR₂): Contain nitrogen. Primary (1°): R-NH₂, Secondary (2°): R₂NH, Tertiary (3°): R₃N. Naming: -amine suffix. Examples: Methylamine (CH₃NH₂), Aniline (C₆H₅NH₂). Basic (accept H⁺, form salts), can hydrogen bond. Important in: Amino acids (proteins), neurotransmitters (dopamine, serotonin), pharmaceuticals, dyes.
  6. 6.
    Amides (-CONH₂, -CONHR, -CONR₂): Contain carbonyl bonded to nitrogen. Formed from acid + amine. Naming: -amide suffix. Examples: Acetamide, peptides, proteins. Can hydrogen bond (strong), relatively unreactive. Important in: Proteins (peptide bonds), synthetic polymers (nylons), pharmaceuticals.
  7. 7.
    Ethers (-O-): Oxygen between carbons. Naming: Alkyl-alkyl ether. Examples: Diethyl ether (solvent, anesthetic), THF (tetrahydrofuran, solvent). Polar but cannot hydrogen bond (lower boiling points than alcohols), good solvents. Important in: Solvents, anesthetics, starting materials.
  8. 8.
    Halides (-X, X = F, Cl, Br, I): Halogen substituents. Naming: Halo- prefix. Examples: Chloroform (CHCl₃), Teflon (poly(tetrafluoroethylene)). Important in: Pharmaceuticals, polymers, refrigerants (CFCs, now phased out), solvents.

Isomerism in Organic Chemistry:

  • • Structural Isomers: Same molecular formula, different connectivity. Different chemical and physical properties. Examples: Butane vs. 2-methylpropane (branched), propanol vs. methoxyethane.
  • • Stereoisomers: Same connectivity, different spatial arrangement. Geometric isomers (cis/trans, E/Z): Different arrangement around double bond or ring. Enantiomers: Mirror images (chiral molecules, not superimposable), rotate plane-polarized light in opposite directions, identical physical properties except optical activity. Diastereomers: Non-mirror-image stereoisomers, different physical properties.
  • • Chirality: Molecules not superimposable on their mirror images. Have chiral centers (carbon with 4 different substituents). Enantiomers have identical properties except interaction with polarized light and chiral environments (e.g., biological systems). One enantiomer may be biologically active, other inactive or harmful. Important in: Pharmaceuticals (one enantiomer may be drug, other may be harmful), biological molecules (amino acids, sugars usually one enantiomer).

Acids, Bases, and pH

Acids and bases are fundamental to chemistry, biology, and everyday life. Understanding acid-base chemistry is essential for understanding biological processes, environmental chemistry, and many industrial applications.

Acid-Base Definitions:

  • • Arrhenius Definition: Acids produce H⁺ (H₃O⁺ in water) when dissolved. Bases produce OH⁻ when dissolved. Limited to aqueous solutions. Example: HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻ (acid), NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻ (base).
  • • Bronsted-Lowry Definition: Acids donate protons (H⁺), bases accept protons. More general than Arrhenius. Acid-base pairs: Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by one proton. Example: NH₃ (base) + H₂O (acid) ⇌ NH₄⁺ (conjugate acid) + OH⁻ (conjugate base). Amphoteric substances can act as acid or base (e.g., water, HCO₃⁻).
  • • Lewis Definition: Acids accept electron pairs, bases donate electron pairs. Most general definition. Includes species without protons. Example: BF₃ (Lewis acid, electron-deficient) + NH₃ (Lewis base, lone pair donor) → F₃B-NH₃ (adduct).

pH Scale and Calculations:

  • • pH Scale: Logarithmic scale measuring acidity: pH = -log[H⁺]. Range: 0-14 (in water). pH < 7: Acidic ([H⁺] > [OH⁻]). pH = 7: Neutral ([H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷ M). pH > 7: Basic/Alkaline ([H⁺] < [OH⁻]). Each pH unit represents 10× change in [H⁺]. pOH = -log[OH⁻]. pH + pOH = 14 (at 25°C).
  • • Strong vs. Weak Acids/Bases: Strong acids/bases: Completely dissociate in water. Strong acids: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HBr, HI, HClO₄. Strong bases: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂. Weak acids/bases: Partially dissociate, establish equilibrium. Weak acids: CH₃COOH, H₂CO₃, H₃PO₄. Weak bases: NH₃, CH₃NH₂. Ka (acid dissociation constant) and Kb (base dissociation constant) measure strength.
  • • Acid-Base Reactions (Neutralization): Acid + Base → Salt + Water. Example: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O. Equivalence point: Moles acid = moles base. Can be titrated to determine concentration. Indicators change color at endpoint (near equivalence point).

Buffers:

  • • Buffer Solutions: Resist pH changes when small amounts of acid or base added. Contain weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base and its conjugate acid). Buffer capacity: Amount of acid/base that can be added without significant pH change. Example: CH₃COOH/CH₃COO⁻ (acetic acid/sodium acetate).
  • • Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]), where [A⁻] = conjugate base concentration, [HA] = weak acid concentration. Used to calculate buffer pH. When [A⁻] = [HA]: pH = pKa (maximum buffer capacity). Effective buffer range: pH = pKa ± 1.
  • • Biological Importance: Blood buffers maintain pH ~7.4 (HCO₃⁻/H₂CO₃ system). Cellular pH regulation critical for enzyme function, protein structure, metabolic processes. Buffers prevent dangerous pH swings in biological systems.

Solutions and Solubility

Solutions are homogeneous mixtures of two or more substances. Understanding solutions and solubility is essential for understanding many chemical and biological processes.

Solution Properties:

  • • Solution Components: Solvent: Component present in greater amount (usually liquid, e.g., water). Solute: Component present in smaller amount, dissolves in solvent. Homogeneous mixture: Uniform composition throughout.
  • • Concentration Units: Molarity (M): Moles solute / Liters solution. Molality (m): Moles solute / Kilograms solvent. Mass percent: (Mass solute / Mass solution) × 100%. Mole fraction (X): Moles component / Total moles. Parts per million (ppm): Mass solute / Mass solution × 10⁶. Used for very dilute solutions.
  • • Solubility: Maximum amount of solute that dissolves in given amount of solvent at specific temperature. Saturated solution: Contains maximum dissolved solute at equilibrium. Unsaturated: Less than maximum, can dissolve more. Supersaturated: More than maximum (unstable, metastable). Like dissolves like: Polar solutes dissolve in polar solvents, nonpolar in nonpolar.

Colligative Properties:

Properties depending on number of solute particles, not their identity. Caused by decreased solvent concentration.

  • • Vapor Pressure Lowering: Addition of nonvolatile solute lowers vapor pressure of solvent (Raoult's law). Vapor pressure = X_solvent × P°_solvent, where X = mole fraction, P° = pure solvent vapor pressure. Explains why salt solutions evaporate slower than pure water.
  • • Boiling Point Elevation: Solution boils at higher temperature than pure solvent. ΔTb = Kb × m × i, where Kb = molal boiling point constant, m = molality, i = van't Hoff factor (number of particles per formula unit). Example: Salt water boils above 100°C.
  • • Freezing Point Depression: Solution freezes at lower temperature than pure solvent. ΔTf = Kf × m × i, where Kf = molal freezing point constant. Example: Antifreeze in car radiators (ethylene glycol lowers freezing point), salt on icy roads melts ice.
  • • Osmotic Pressure: Pressure needed to prevent osmosis (net movement of solvent through semipermeable membrane). π = MRT × i, where π = osmotic pressure, M = molarity, R = gas constant, T = temperature. Critical in biological systems (cell membranes, kidney function). Hypertonic: Higher solute concentration. Hypotonic: Lower solute concentration. Isotonic: Equal concentrations.
