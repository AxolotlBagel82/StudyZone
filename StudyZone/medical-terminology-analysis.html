Medical Terminology: In Depth Analysis

Comprehensive guide to healthcare language

Word Building System

Medical terminology is a specialized language based primarily on Greek and Latin roots, prefixes, and suffixes. This systematic approach allows healthcare professionals to communicate precisely about anatomy, physiology, diseases, procedures, and treatments. Understanding word construction enables you to decode unfamiliar terms and build new ones. Most medical terms follow a predictable pattern: prefix (optional) + root word + suffix. Combining vowels (usually "o" or "i") connect word parts when needed.

Root Words (Stems) - Core Meanings:

  • •Cardi/o: Heart. Examples: cardiology (study of heart), cardiac (pertaining to heart), cardiologist (heart specialist), cardiomegaly (enlarged heart), electrocardiogram (ECG, heart electrical recording), pericardium (membrane around heart).
  • •Derm/o, Dermat/o: Skin. Examples: dermatology (study of skin), dermatitis (skin inflammation), dermatologist (skin specialist), epidermis (outer skin layer), hypodermic (under skin), dermatome (skin area supplied by single nerve).
  • •Gastr/o: Stomach. Examples: gastritis (stomach inflammation), gastroenterology (study of stomach and intestines), gastric (pertaining to stomach), gastrectomy (stomach removal), gastroscopy (stomach examination), gastroesophageal (stomach and esophagus).
  • •Hepat/o: Liver. Examples: hepatitis (liver inflammation), hepatology (study of liver), hepatic (pertaining to liver), hepatomegaly (enlarged liver), hepatocyte (liver cell), hepatectomy (liver removal).
  • •Nephr/o, Ren/o: Kidney. Examples: nephritis (kidney inflammation), nephrology (study of kidneys), nephron (kidney functional unit), nephrectomy (kidney removal), renal (pertaining to kidney), renin (kidney hormone).
  • •Oste/o: Bone. Examples: osteoporosis (porous bones), osteology (study of bones), osteocyte (bone cell), osteotomy (bone incision), osteoarthritis (bone and joint inflammation), osteoblast (bone-building cell).
  • •Pulmon/o, Pneum/o, Pneumon/o: Lung. Examples: pulmonary (pertaining to lungs), pulmonology (study of lungs), pneumonia (lung inflammation), pneumothorax (air in chest cavity), pneumonectomy (lung removal), respiratory (breathing).
  • •Neur/o: Nerve. Examples: neurology (study of nerves), neuron (nerve cell), neuropathy (nerve disease), neuralgia (nerve pain), neurosurgeon (nerve surgeon), neurological (pertaining to nerves).
  • •My/o: Muscle. Examples: myology (study of muscles), myopathy (muscle disease), myalgia (muscle pain), myocardium (heart muscle), myositis (muscle inflammation), myectomy (muscle removal).
  • •Arthr/o: Joint. Examples: arthritis (joint inflammation), arthroscopy (joint examination), arthroplasty (joint repair), arthralgia (joint pain), arthropathy (joint disease), arthrocentesis (joint fluid removal).
  • •Enter/o: Intestine. Examples: enteritis (intestine inflammation), gastroenterology (stomach and intestine study), enterostomy (intestine opening), enteropathy (intestine disease), enteral (pertaining to intestine).
  • •Col/o, Colon/o: Colon. Examples: colitis (colon inflammation), colonoscopy (colon examination), colectomy (colon removal), colorectal (colon and rectum), colostomy (colon opening).
  • •Hemat/o, Hem/o: Blood. Examples: hematology (study of blood), hematoma (blood collection), hemorrhage (blood loss), hemoglobin (blood protein), hemophilia (bleeding disorder), hematopoiesis (blood cell formation).
  • •Angi/o, Vas/o: Vessel. Examples: angiology (study of vessels), angiography (vessel imaging), vasodilation (vessel widening), vasoconstriction (vessel narrowing), angioplasty (vessel repair).
  • •Crani/o: Skull. Examples: craniotomy (skull incision), cranium (skull), cranial (pertaining to skull), craniectomy (skull removal), intracranial (within skull).
  • •Thorac/o, Pector/o: Chest. Examples: thoracic (pertaining to chest), thoracotomy (chest incision), thorax (chest), pectoral (pertaining to chest), thoracoscopy (chest examination).
  • •Abdomin/o, Lapar/o: Abdomen. Examples: abdominal (pertaining to abdomen), laparotomy (abdomen incision), laparoscopy (abdomen examination), abdominoplasty (abdomen repair).
  • •Urethr/o: Urethra. Examples: urethritis (urethra inflammation), urethral (pertaining to urethra), urethroplasty (urethra repair), urethroscopy (urethra examination).
  • •Cyst/o: Bladder, cyst. Examples: cystitis (bladder inflammation), cystoscopy (bladder examination), cystectomy (bladder removal), cystolith (bladder stone).

Combining Forms and Vowels:

  • •Combining Vowel: Usually "o" (sometimes "i" or "e") connects word parts. Used when suffix begins with consonant (gastr/o + -scopy = gastroscopy). Omitted when suffix begins with vowel (gastr + -itis = gastritis). Not used between two word roots (gastroenterology: gastr/o + enter/o + -ology).
  • •Word Analysis: Break terms into components: prefix + root + suffix. Example: "hypertension" = hyper- (excessive) + tens (pressure) + -ion (condition) = excessive blood pressure. "Gastroenteritis" = gastr/o (stomach) + enter/o (intestine) + -itis (inflammation) = inflammation of stomach and intestines.

Common Prefixes

Prefixes are word parts added to the beginning of root words to modify their meaning. They typically indicate location, direction, quantity, size, or time. Understanding prefixes is crucial for interpreting medical terms and understanding relationships between conditions.

Quantity and Size Prefixes:

  • •Hyper-: Excessive, above normal, increased. Examples: hypertension (high blood pressure), hyperglycemia (high blood sugar), hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid), hyperthermia (elevated body temperature), hyperactive (excessively active), hyperventilation (rapid breathing).
  • •Hypo-: Deficient, below normal, decreased. Examples: hypotension (low blood pressure), hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid), hypothermia (low body temperature), hypovolemia (low blood volume), hypoxia (low oxygen).
  • •Poly-: Many, much, excessive. Examples: polyuria (excessive urination), polydipsia (excessive thirst), polyphagia (excessive eating), polyarthritis (multiple joint inflammation), polycythemia (excess red blood cells).
  • •Oligo-: Few, scanty, little. Examples: oliguria (scanty urination), oligomenorrhea (infrequent menstruation), oligospermia (low sperm count), oligodendrocyte (few-branched nerve cell).
  • •Mono-: One, single. Examples: monocyte (single-nucleus white blood cell), mononucleosis (single-nucleus condition), monoplegia (paralysis of one limb), monotherapy (single treatment).
  • •Multi-: Many, multiple. Examples: multinodular (multiple nodules), multiorgan (multiple organs), multipara (multiple births), multivitamin (multiple vitamins).
  • •Mega-, Megalo-: Large, enlarged. Examples: megacolon (enlarged colon), megalocardia (enlarged heart), megalocyte (large cell), megakaryocyte (large nucleus cell).
  • •Micro-: Small. Examples: microorganism (small organism), microcyte (small cell), microsurgery (small-scale surgery), microscope (instrument for viewing small objects).

Time and Position Prefixes:

  • •Pre-: Before, in front of. Examples: prenatal (before birth), preoperative (before surgery), premature (before maturity), premenstrual (before menstruation), precancerous (before cancer), prediabetes (before diabetes).
  • •Post-: After, behind. Examples: postoperative (after surgery), postpartum (after childbirth), postmortem (after death), postprandial (after meals), posttraumatic (after trauma), postmenopausal (after menopause).
  • •Peri-: Around, surrounding. Examples: pericardium (around heart), peritoneum (around abdomen), perianal (around anus), periorbital (around eye), perinatal (around birth).
  • •Circum-: Around. Examples: circumcision (cutting around), circumference (distance around), circumoral (around mouth), circumrenal (around kidney).

Location and Direction Prefixes:

  • •Sub-: Under, below, beneath. Examples: subcutaneous (under skin), sublingual (under tongue), subclavian (under clavicle), subdural (under dura mater), submandibular (under mandible), subcostal (under ribs).
  • •Supra-: Above, over, superior. Examples: suprarenal (above kidney/adrenal), suprapubic (above pubis), supraclavicular (above clavicle), suprascapular (above scapula).
  • •Infra-: Below, beneath, inferior. Examples: infraorbital (below orbit), infrapatellar (below patella), infraclavicular (below clavicle), infrascapular (below scapula).
  • •Intra-: Within, inside. Examples: intravenous (within vein), intramuscular (within muscle), intracranial (within skull), intrauterine (within uterus), intradermal (within skin), intraarticular (within joint).
  • •Inter-: Between, among. Examples: intercostal (between ribs), intervertebral (between vertebrae), intercellular (between cells), intermuscular (between muscles), interdigital (between digits/fingers).
  • •Trans-: Across, through, beyond. Examples: transfusion (transfer across), transplant (move across), transdermal (through skin), transurethral (through urethra), transabdominal (across abdomen), transesophageal (through esophagus).
  • •Epi-: Upon, on, over, above. Examples: epidermis (upon skin), epigastric (above stomach), epicardium (upon heart), epinephrine (upon kidney/adrenal), epiphysis (upon bone).
  • •Endo-: Within, inside, inner. Examples: endocrine (internal secretion), endocardium (inner heart lining), endometrium (inner uterus lining), endoscopy (internal examination), endosteum (inner bone lining).
  • •Ecto-, Exo-: Outside, external, outer. Examples: ectopic (outside normal location), exocrine (external secretion), exoskeleton (external skeleton), exophthalmos (protruding eyes).
  • •Para-: Beside, near, alongside, abnormal. Examples: parathyroid (beside thyroid), paranasal (beside nose), paracervical (beside cervix), paralysis (abnormal function), paranoia (abnormal thinking).
  • •Retro-: Behind, backward, back. Examples: retroperitoneal (behind peritoneum), retrograde (backward), retrosternal (behind sternum), retroversion (backward turning).

Condition and Quality Prefixes:

  • •A-, An-: Without, absence of, not. Examples: aphasia (without speech), anemia (without blood/hemoglobin), anuria (without urination), anorexia (without appetite), analgesia (without pain), aseptic (without infection).
  • •Dys-: Bad, difficult, painful, abnormal. Examples: dyspnea (difficult breathing), dysphagia (difficult swallowing), dysuria (painful urination), dysfunction (abnormal function), dystrophy (abnormal development), dysrhythmia (abnormal rhythm).
  • •Eu-: Good, normal, well, easy. Examples: eupnea (normal breathing), euthyroid (normal thyroid), euphoria (good feeling), euglycemia (normal blood sugar).
  • •Mal-: Bad, poor, abnormal. Examples: malabsorption (poor absorption), malnutrition (poor nutrition), malformation (abnormal formation), malalignment (poor alignment).
  • •Brady-: Slow. Examples: bradycardia (slow heart rate), bradypnea (slow breathing), bradykinesia (slow movement), bradyarrhythmia (slow irregular rhythm).
  • •Tachy-: Fast, rapid. Examples: tachycardia (fast heart rate), tachypnea (rapid breathing), tachyarrhythmia (fast irregular rhythm), tachyphagia (rapid eating).
  • •Pseudo-: False, fake. Examples: pseudocyst (false cyst), pseudoparalysis (false paralysis), pseudomembrane (false membrane), pseudoscience (false science).

Common Suffixes

Suffixes are word parts added to the end of root words to modify meaning or indicate condition, procedure, or specialty. They often determine whether a term is a noun, adjective, or describes a process. Suffixes can indicate diseases, procedures, conditions, or convert words to different grammatical forms.

Disease and Condition Suffixes:

  • •-itis: Inflammation. Examples: arthritis (joint inflammation), gastritis (stomach inflammation), hepatitis (liver inflammation), bronchitis (bronchial inflammation), appendicitis (appendix inflammation), dermatitis (skin inflammation), tonsillitis (tonsil inflammation), neuritis (nerve inflammation).
  • •-osis: Condition, usually abnormal, process. Examples: osteoporosis (porous bone condition), thrombosis (blood clot condition), cyanosis (blue condition), neurosis (nerve condition), fibrosis (fibrous tissue condition), stenosis (narrowing condition), necrosis (death condition).
  • •-pathy: Disease, suffering, feeling. Examples: neuropathy (nerve disease), cardiomyopathy (heart muscle disease), myopathy (muscle disease), retinopathy (retina disease), nephropathy (kidney disease), encephalopathy (brain disease), arthropathy (joint disease).
  • •-algia, -dynia: Pain. Examples: neuralgia (nerve pain), myalgia (muscle pain), arthralgia (joint pain), cephalgia (head pain/headache), otalgia (ear pain), gastrodynia (stomach pain).
  • •-malacia: Softening. Examples: osteomalacia (bone softening), chondromalacia (cartilage softening), encephalomalacia (brain softening), keratomalacia (cornea softening).
  • •-sclerosis: Hardening. Examples: atherosclerosis (artery hardening), arteriosclerosis (artery hardening), multiple sclerosis (multiple hardenings), nephrosclerosis (kidney hardening).
  • •-megaly: Enlargement. Examples: cardiomegaly (heart enlargement), hepatomegaly (liver enlargement), splenomegaly (spleen enlargement), acromegaly (extremity enlargement).
  • •-emia: Blood condition. Examples: anemia (without/low blood), leukemia (white blood), hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), hyperglycemia (high blood sugar), uremia (urine in blood), bacteremia (bacteria in blood), septicemia (infection in blood).
  • •-uria: Urine condition. Examples: polyuria (excessive urination), oliguria (scanty urination), anuria (no urination), hematuria (blood in urine), proteinuria (protein in urine), glycosuria (sugar in urine), pyuria (pus in urine).
  • •-penia: Deficiency, decrease. Examples: leukopenia (low white blood cells), thrombocytopenia (low platelets), neutropenia (low neutrophils), erythropenia (low red blood cells), pancytopenia (low all blood cells).
  • •-phagia: Eating, swallowing. Examples: dysphagia (difficult swallowing), polyphagia (excessive eating), aphagia (inability to swallow), odynophagia (painful swallowing).
  • •-phobia: Fear. Examples: claustrophobia (fear of enclosed spaces), agoraphobia (fear of open spaces), hydrophobia (fear of water/rabies), hemophobia (fear of blood).

Surgical Procedure Suffixes:

  • •-ectomy: Surgical removal, excision. Examples: appendectomy (appendix removal), tonsillectomy (tonsil removal), hysterectomy (uterus removal), cholecystectomy (gallbladder removal), mastectomy (breast removal), nephrectomy (kidney removal), gastrectomy (stomach removal), colectomy (colon removal).
  • •-otomy: Surgical incision, cutting into. Examples: tracheotomy (windpipe incision), laparotomy (abdomen incision), craniotomy (skull incision), thoracotomy (chest incision), arthrotomy (joint incision), phlebotomy (vein incision/blood draw).
  • •-ostomy: Surgical creation of opening. Examples: colostomy (colon opening), ileostomy (ileum opening), tracheostomy (trachea opening), gastrostomy (stomach opening), cystostomy (bladder opening), nephrostomy (kidney opening).
  • •-plasty: Surgical repair, reconstruction. Examples: rhinoplasty (nose repair), angioplasty (vessel repair), arthroplasty (joint repair), mammoplasty (breast repair), otoplasty (ear repair), blepharoplasty (eyelid repair).
  • •-rrhaphy: Suture, repair. Examples: herniorrhaphy (hernia repair), tenorrhaphy (tendon repair), myorrhaphy (muscle repair), neurorrhaphy (nerve repair).
  • •-pexy: Surgical fixation, suspension. Examples: nephropexy (kidney fixation), hysteropexy (uterus fixation), orchiopexy (testis fixation), gastropexy (stomach fixation).
  • •-desis: Binding, fusion. Examples: arthrodesis (joint fusion), spondylodesis (vertebra fusion), syndesmosis (ligament binding).
  • •-centesis: Surgical puncture to remove fluid. Examples: arthrocentesis (joint fluid removal), paracentesis (abdominal fluid removal), thoracentesis (chest fluid removal), amniocentesis (amniotic fluid removal), pericardiocentesis (heart sac fluid removal).

Diagnostic and Examination Suffixes:

  • •-scopy: Visual examination, viewing. Examples: endoscopy (internal examination), gastroscopy (stomach examination), colonoscopy (colon examination), laparoscopy (abdomen examination), arthroscopy (joint examination), cystoscopy (bladder examination), bronchoscopy (bronchial examination).
  • •-graphy: Process of recording, writing. Examples: radiography (X-ray recording), mammography (breast imaging), angiography (vessel imaging), electrocardiography (heart electrical recording), encephalography (brain recording).
  • •-gram: Record, recording. Examples: electrocardiogram (ECG, heart electrical record), mammogram (breast X-ray record), angiogram (vessel X-ray record), sonogram (ultrasound record), encephalogram (brain electrical record).
  • •-graph: Instrument for recording. Examples: electrocardiograph (ECG machine), sphygmomanograph (blood pressure recorder), thermograph (temperature recorder).

Specialty and Study Suffixes:

  • •-ology: Study of, science of. Examples: cardiology (study of heart), dermatology (study of skin), neurology (study of nerves), pathology (study of disease), psychology (study of mind), oncology (study of tumors), urology (study of urinary system), gastroenterology (study of stomach and intestines).
  • •-ist: Specialist, one who practices. Examples: cardiologist (heart specialist), dermatologist (skin specialist), neurologist (nerve specialist), pathologist (disease specialist), psychologist (mind specialist), oncologist (cancer specialist).
  • •-ian, -ician: Specialist. Examples: physician (medical specialist), pediatrician (child specialist), technician (technical specialist), clinician (clinical specialist).

Descriptive and Adjective Suffixes:

  • •-ic, -ac: Pertaining to. Examples: cardiac (pertaining to heart), gastric (pertaining to stomach), hepatic (pertaining to liver), renal (pertaining to kidney), neural (pertaining to nerve), pulmonary (pertaining to lung), thoracic (pertaining to chest).
  • •-al, -eal, -ial: Pertaining to. Examples: abdominal (pertaining to abdomen), intestinal (pertaining to intestine), arterial (pertaining to artery), venal (pertaining to vein), cranial (pertaining to skull), spinal (pertaining to spine).
  • •-ous, -ious: Pertaining to, full of. Examples: nervous (pertaining to nerves), mucous (pertaining to mucus), serous (pertaining to serum), infectious (pertaining to infection), contagious (pertaining to contact).
  • •-oid: Resembling, like. Examples: rheumatoid (resembling rheumatism), fibroid (resembling fiber), sigmoid (resembling S), thyroid (resembling shield), lipoid (resembling fat).
  • •-form: Resembling, shaped like. Examples: uniform (one shape), multiform (many shapes), cruciform (cross-shaped), fusiform (spindle-shaped).
  • •-ia, -y: Condition, state. Examples: pneumonia (lung condition), anemia (blood condition), dysuria (urination condition), ataxia (coordination condition), dystrophy (development condition).

Body Systems Terminology

Medical terminology is organized by body systems, each with specific root words, prefixes, and suffixes. Understanding system-specific terminology is essential for accurate communication in healthcare settings. Terms often describe anatomical structures, physiological functions, pathological conditions, diagnostic procedures, and treatments within each system.

Cardiovascular System (Circulatory):

  • •Cardiomegaly: Enlarged heart (cardi/o + -megaly). Often indicates heart failure, hypertension, or valvular disease.
  • •Bradycardia: Slow heart rate, typically <60 bpm (brady- + cardi/o + -ia). Can be normal in athletes or indicate heart block, medication effects, or hypothyroidism.
  • •Tachycardia: Fast heart rate, typically >100 bpm (tachy- + cardi/o + -ia). May indicate stress, fever, arrhythmias, or heart disease.
  • •Myocardial Infarction (MI): Heart attack, death of heart muscle due to blocked coronary artery (myocardi/o + -al, infarct + -ion). Critical emergency requiring immediate treatment.
  • •Arrhythmia: Abnormal heart rhythm (a- + rhythm + -ia). Includes atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia, bradyarrhythmias.
  • •Hypertension: High blood pressure (hyper- + tens + -ion). Primary (essential) or secondary (caused by another condition).
  • •Hypotension: Low blood pressure (hypo- + tens + -ion). May cause dizziness, fainting, shock.
  • •Atherosclerosis: Hardening/narrowing of arteries due to plaque (athero- + scler/o + -osis). Leading cause of heart disease and stroke.
  • •Angina Pectoris: Chest pain due to reduced blood flow to heart (angina = pain, pectoris = chest). Often occurs with exertion.
  • •Cardiac Catheterization: Procedure to examine heart and blood vessels (cardi/o + -ac, catheter + -ization). Used for diagnosis and treatment.
  • •Coronary Artery Bypass Graft (CABG): Surgical procedure to bypass blocked coronary arteries (coronary = crown-like, artery, bypass, graft).
  • •Angioplasty: Vessel repair, typically balloon dilation of narrowed artery (angi/o + -plasty). Often includes stent placement.
  • •Thrombosis: Blood clot formation (thromb/o + -osis). Deep vein thrombosis (DVT), coronary thrombosis.
  • •Embolism: Blockage of vessel by traveling clot or foreign material (embol + -ism). Pulmonary embolism (PE) is life-threatening.

Respiratory System:

  • •Pneumonia: Lung inflammation, usually infectious (pneumon/o + -ia). Can be bacterial, viral, or fungal. Community-acquired or hospital-acquired.
  • •Bronchitis: Bronchial tube inflammation (bronch/o + -itis). Acute (short-term, often viral) or chronic (long-term, often from smoking).
  • •Dyspnea: Difficulty breathing, shortness of breath (dys- + -pnea). Common symptom of respiratory, cardiac, or other conditions.
  • •Eupnea: Normal breathing (eu- + -pnea). Baseline respiratory rate 12-20 breaths/min in adults.
  • •Apnea: Absence of breathing (a- + -pnea). Sleep apnea (repeated episodes during sleep), respiratory arrest.
  • •Tachypnea: Rapid breathing (tachy- + -pnea). >20 breaths/min, may indicate fever, anxiety, or respiratory distress.
  • •Bradypnea: Slow breathing (brady- + -pnea). <12 breaths/min, may indicate drug overdose, brain injury, or metabolic issues.
  • •Asthma: Chronic inflammatory airway disease causing reversible bronchospasm. Characterized by wheezing, dyspnea, chest tightness.
  • •Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD): Progressive lung disease including emphysema and chronic bronchitis. Usually caused by smoking.
  • •Emphysema: Destruction of alveoli, causing air trapping (emphysema = inflation). Type of COPD, leads to barrel chest, dyspnea.
  • •Pneumothorax: Air in pleural space, collapsed lung (pneum/o + thorax). Spontaneous or traumatic, requires chest tube.
  • •Pleural Effusion: Fluid accumulation in pleural space (pleur/o + -al, effusion). May be transudate or exudate, causes dyspnea.
  • •Tracheostomy: Surgical opening in trachea (trache/o + -ostomy). Provides airway, used for long-term ventilation or airway obstruction.
  • •Bronchoscopy: Visual examination of bronchi (bronch/o + -scopy). Used for diagnosis, biopsy, or removal of foreign bodies.
  • •Pulmonary Embolism (PE): Blockage of pulmonary artery by clot (pulmon/o + -ary, embol + -ism). Life-threatening, causes sudden dyspnea, chest pain.

Digestive System (Gastrointestinal):

  • •Gastritis: Stomach inflammation (gastr/o + -itis). Acute or chronic, caused by H. pylori, NSAIDs, alcohol, stress.
  • •Gastroenteritis: Stomach and intestine inflammation (gastr/o + enter/o + -itis). "Stomach flu," causes nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.
  • •Hepatitis: Liver inflammation (hepat/o + -itis). Types: A (viral, food/water), B (blood/sexual), C (blood), alcoholic, autoimmune.
  • •Cholecystectomy: Gallbladder removal (cholecyst/o + -ectomy). Common for gallstones (cholelithiasis), can be laparoscopic or open.
  • •Cholelithiasis: Gallstones (chole- + lith + -iasis). Can cause biliary colic, cholecystitis, or be asymptomatic.
  • •Colitis: Colon inflammation (col/o + -itis). Types: ulcerative colitis (UC), Crohn's disease, infectious, ischemic.
  • •Appendicitis: Appendix inflammation (appendic/o + -itis). Surgical emergency, requires appendectomy to prevent rupture.
  • •Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD): Chronic acid reflux (gastr/o + esophag/o + -eal, reflux). Causes heartburn, can lead to Barrett's esophagus.
  • •Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD): Ulcers in stomach or duodenum (pept/o + -ic, ulcer). Caused by H. pylori or NSAIDs.
  • •Diverticulitis: Inflammation of diverticula (pouches) in colon (diverticul/o + -itis). Causes abdominal pain, fever, can lead to perforation.
  • •Ileus: Intestinal obstruction or paralysis (ile/o + -us). Paralytic ileus (functional) or mechanical obstruction.
  • •Colonoscopy: Visual examination of colon (colon/o + -scopy). Screening for polyps and cancer, diagnostic for bleeding or symptoms.
  • •Endoscopy: Internal examination, typically upper GI (end/o + -scopy). Gastroscopy examines esophagus, stomach, duodenum.
  • •Laparoscopy: Abdominal examination through small incisions (lapar/o + -scopy). Minimally invasive surgery, faster recovery.
  • •Hernia: Protrusion of organ through wall. Types: inguinal, hiatal, umbilical, incisional. May require herniorrhaphy (repair).

Nervous System:

  • •Neuralgia: Nerve pain (neur/o + -algia). Trigeminal neuralgia, postherpetic neuralgia (after shingles).
  • •Neuropathy: Nerve disease (neur/o + -pathy). Peripheral neuropathy (diabetic, alcoholic), autonomic neuropathy.
  • •Encephalitis: Brain inflammation (encephal/o + -itis). Viral (herpes, West Nile) or bacterial, causes fever, headache, confusion.
  • •Meningitis: Meninges (brain/spinal cord covering) inflammation (mening/o + -itis). Bacterial (life-threatening) or viral, causes stiff neck, fever.
  • •Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident, CVA): Brain damage from interrupted blood flow. Ischemic (clot) or hemorrhagic (bleeding).
  • •Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA): Temporary stroke symptoms (transient, isch/o + -emia, attack). "Mini-stroke," warning sign of future stroke.
  • •Epilepsy: Recurrent seizures (epilepsy = seizure). Various types: generalized, partial, absence seizures.
  • •Paralysis: Loss of muscle function (para- + -lysis). Hemiplegia (one side), paraplegia (lower body), quadriplegia (all limbs).
  • •Paresis: Partial paralysis, weakness (paresis = letting go). Hemiparesis (one-side weakness), monoparesis (one-limb weakness).
  • •Dementia: Progressive cognitive decline (de- + ment + -ia). Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia.
  • •Multiple Sclerosis (MS): Autoimmune demyelinating disease (multiple, scler/o + -osis). Causes neurological symptoms, relapses and remissions.
  • •Parkinson's Disease: Progressive movement disorder. Characterized by tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia (slow movement), postural instability.
  • •Electroencephalogram (EEG): Brain electrical activity recording (electr/o + encephal/o + -gram). Used for epilepsy diagnosis, brain death determination.
  • •Lumbar Puncture (LP): Spinal tap, CSF collection from lumbar spine (lumb/o + -ar, puncture). Diagnoses meningitis, measures CSF pressure.

Musculoskeletal System:

  • •Arthritis: Joint inflammation (arthr/o + -itis). Osteoarthritis (wear-and-tear), rheumatoid arthritis (autoimmune), gout (crystal deposition).
  • •Osteoporosis: Porous bones, decreased bone density (oste/o + por/o + -osis). Risk of fractures, common in postmenopausal women.
  • •Osteoarthritis (OA): Degenerative joint disease (oste/o + arthr/o + -itis). "Wear-and-tear" arthritis, most common type.
  • •Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA): Autoimmune joint disease (rheumat/o + -oid, arthr/o + -itis). Symmetric joint involvement, systemic disease.
  • •Myalgia: Muscle pain (my/o + -algia). Common in viral infections, fibromyalgia, overuse injuries.
  • •Myositis: Muscle inflammation (my/o + -itis). Polymyositis (multiple muscles), dermatomyositis (muscle and skin).
  • •Fracture: Broken bone. Types: closed (simple), open (compound), comminuted (shattered), greenstick (incomplete, children).
  • •Dislocation: Joint displacement (dis- + locat + -ion). Shoulder, hip, finger dislocations require reduction (realignment).
  • •Sprain: Ligament injury (stretch or tear). Ankle, knee, wrist sprains common in sports.
  • •Strain: Muscle or tendon injury (stretch or tear). Back strain, hamstring strain.
  • •Arthroscopy: Joint examination (arthr/o + -scopy). Minimally invasive, used for diagnosis and treatment (meniscus repair, ACL reconstruction).
  • •Arthroplasty: Joint replacement (arthr/o + -plasty). Total hip replacement (THR), total knee replacement (TKR).
  • •Osteomyelitis: Bone infection (oste/o + myel/o + -itis). Usually bacterial, requires antibiotics, may need surgical debridement.

Urinary System:

  • •Nephritis: Kidney inflammation (nephr/o + -itis). Glomerulonephritis (glomeruli), pyelonephritis (kidney and renal pelvis, usually bacterial).
  • •Cystitis: Bladder inflammation (cyst/o + -itis). Usually bacterial (UTI), causes dysuria, frequency, urgency.
  • •Urethritis: Urethra inflammation (urethr/o + -itis). Can be infectious (gonorrhea, chlamydia) or non-infectious.
  • •Nephrolithiasis: Kidney stones (nephr/o + lith + -iasis). Renal calculi, can cause severe pain (renal colic), hematuria.
  • •Urolithiasis: Stones in urinary tract (ur/o + lith + -iasis). Includes kidney, ureter, bladder stones.
  • •Renal Failure: Kidney function loss (ren/o + -al, failure). Acute (sudden) or chronic (progressive), may require dialysis or transplant.
  • •Hematuria: Blood in urine (hemat/o + -uria). Gross (visible) or microscopic, can indicate infection, stones, cancer, trauma.
  • •Proteinuria: Protein in urine (protein + -uria). Indicates kidney damage, nephrotic syndrome, or other conditions.
  • •Dysuria: Painful urination (dys- + -uria). Common in UTIs, urethritis, bladder infections.
  • •Polyuria: Excessive urination (poly- + -uria). Diabetes, diuretics, kidney disease.
  • •Oliguria: Scanty urination (olig/o + -uria). <400ml/day, indicates kidney dysfunction, dehydration, obstruction.
  • •Anuria: No urination (an- + -uria). <100ml/day, severe kidney failure, obstruction, critical condition.
  • •Incontinence: Loss of bladder control (in- + continence). Stress, urge, overflow, functional incontinence.
  • •Dialysis: Artificial kidney function (dia- + -lysis). Hemodialysis (blood) or peritoneal dialysis (abdomen), for renal failure.
  • •Cystoscopy: Bladder examination (cyst/o + -scopy). Diagnoses tumors, stones, infections, biopsies tissue.

Integumentary System (Skin):

  • •Dermatitis: Skin inflammation (derm/o + -itis). Contact (irritant/allergic), atopic (eczema), seborrheic.
  • •Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis): Chronic inflammatory skin condition. Itchy, red, scaly patches, common in children.
  • •Psoriasis: Chronic autoimmune skin disease. Red, scaly plaques, often on elbows, knees, scalp.
  • •Cellulitis: Deep skin and subcutaneous tissue infection (cellul/o + -itis). Bacterial, causes redness, warmth, swelling, pain.
  • •Melanoma: Malignant skin cancer from melanocytes (melan/o + -oma). Most dangerous skin cancer, requires early detection.
  • •Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC): Most common skin cancer (bas/o + -al, cell, carcin/o + -oma). Rarely metastasizes, locally invasive.
  • •Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC): Second most common skin cancer (squam/o + -ous, cell, carcin/o + -oma). Can metastasize if advanced.
  • •Ulcer: Open sore, tissue loss. Pressure ulcers (bedsores), venous ulcers, diabetic foot ulcers.
  • •Abscess: Localized pus collection. Skin abscess, requires incision and drainage (I&D).
  • •Biopsy: Tissue sample removal for examination (bio- + -opsy). Punch biopsy, excisional biopsy, needle biopsy.

Directional and Positional Terms

Anatomical directional terms describe the position and relationship of body structures. These terms are standardized and always refer to the anatomical position (standing upright, facing forward, arms at sides, palms forward). Understanding these terms is essential for accurate communication about body location, surgical procedures, and diagnostic findings.

Vertical Direction Terms:

  • •Superior (Cranial, Cephalic): Above, toward head, upper. Examples: The heart is superior to the stomach. The cranial cavity contains the brain. Cephalic refers to head region.
  • •Inferior (Caudal): Below, toward feet, lower. Examples: The stomach is inferior to the heart. The caudal region is toward the tail (coccyx).
  • •Proximal: Nearer to point of attachment or origin. Examples: The elbow is proximal to the wrist. The proximal end of the femur attaches to the hip.
  • •Distal: Farther from point of attachment or origin. Examples: The wrist is distal to the elbow. The distal end of the femur is at the knee.

Front-Back Direction Terms:

  • •Anterior (Ventral): Front, toward front of body. Examples: The sternum is anterior to the heart. Ventral refers to belly side. Anterior view shows front of body.
  • •Posterior (Dorsal): Back, toward back of body. Examples: The spine is posterior to the heart. Dorsal refers to back side. Posterior view shows back of body.

Side-to-Side Direction Terms:

  • •Medial: Toward midline of body, inner. Examples: The heart is medial to the lungs. The medial side of the knee faces the other knee.
  • •Lateral: Away from midline, outer. Examples: The arms are lateral to the trunk. The lateral side of the knee faces outward.
  • •Ipsilateral: Same side of body. Examples: The right arm and right leg are ipsilateral. Ipsilateral hemiplegia affects same side.
  • •Contralateral: Opposite side of body. Examples: Right arm and left leg are contralateral. Contralateral hemiplegia affects opposite side.

Depth Terms:

  • •Superficial: Near surface, external. Examples: The skin is superficial to muscles. Superficial veins are near skin surface.
  • •Deep: Far from surface, internal. Examples: Deep veins are within muscles. Deep structures are protected by superficial layers.

Body Planes:

  • •Sagittal Plane: Vertical plane dividing body into left and right. Midsagittal (median) divides equally. Parasagittal is off-center.
  • •Frontal (Coronal) Plane: Vertical plane dividing body into anterior and posterior (front and back).
  • •Transverse (Horizontal, Axial) Plane: Horizontal plane dividing body into superior and inferior (upper and lower). Cross-sectional view.

Medical Abbreviations and Acronyms

Medical abbreviations and acronyms are used extensively in healthcare documentation to save time and space. However, they can lead to errors if misunderstood. Always verify abbreviations in context, and be aware that some abbreviations have multiple meanings. Many institutions maintain "do not use" lists for dangerous abbreviations.

Common Medical Abbreviations:

  • •Vital Signs: BP (blood pressure), HR (heart rate), RR (respiratory rate), T (temperature), O₂ sat (oxygen saturation), BMI (body mass index).
  • •Routes of Administration: PO (by mouth, per os), IV (intravenous), IM (intramuscular), SC/SQ (subcutaneous), ID (intradermal), PR (per rectum), SL (sublingual), IN (intranasal), TOP (topical).
  • •Frequency: QD (once daily), BID (twice daily), TID (three times daily), QID (four times daily), QHS (at bedtime), PRN (as needed), STAT (immediately), AC (before meals), PC (after meals).
  • •Diagnostic Tests: CBC (complete blood count), CMP (comprehensive metabolic panel), UA (urinalysis), CXR (chest X-ray), CT (computed tomography), MRI (magnetic resonance imaging), ECG/EKG (electrocardiogram), EEG (electroencephalogram).
  • •Conditions: MI (myocardial infarction/heart attack), CVA (cerebrovascular accident/stroke), COPD (chronic obstructive pulmonary disease), CHF (congestive heart failure), DM (diabetes mellitus), HTN (hypertension), UTI (urinary tract infection), DVT (deep vein thrombosis), PE (pulmonary embolism).
  • •Procedures: CABG (coronary artery bypass graft), TURP (transurethral resection of prostate), TAH (total abdominal hysterectomy), ORIF (open reduction internal fixation), I&D (incision and drainage).
  • •Units: mg (milligram), g (gram), kg (kilogram), mL (milliliter), L (liter), mEq (milliequivalent), IU (international unit), % (percent).
  • •Patient Status: NPO (nothing by mouth, nil per os), AMA (against medical advice), DNR (do not resuscitate), DNI (do not intubate), SOB (shortness of breath), DOE (dyspnea on exertion).

Dangerous Abbreviations (Often Prohibited):

  • •U or u: Can be misread as "0" (zero) or "4" (four). Use "units" instead.
  • •IU: Can be misread as "IV" (intravenous) or "10." Use "international units" or spell out.
  • •QD, QOD: Can be misread. Use "daily" or "every other day" instead.
  • •Trailing Zero: 5.0 mg can be misread as 50 mg. Use 5 mg instead.
  • •Missing Leading Zero: .5 mg can be misread as 5 mg. Use 0.5 mg instead.

Medical Specialties and Practitioners

Medical specialties focus on specific body systems, age groups, or types of conditions. Each specialty has its own terminology and abbreviations. Understanding specialty terms helps in communication and documentation within healthcare teams.

Medical Specialties:

  • •Cardiology: Heart and cardiovascular system (cardi/o + -ology). Cardiologist treats heart disease, hypertension, arrhythmias, performs cardiac catheterization.
  • •Pulmonology: Respiratory system (pulmon/o + -ology). Pulmonologist treats asthma, COPD, pneumonia, lung cancer, performs bronchoscopy.
  • •Gastroenterology: Digestive system (gastr/o + enter/o + -ology). Gastroenterologist treats GERD, ulcers, IBD, liver disease, performs endoscopy, colonoscopy.
  • •Nephrology: Kidneys and urinary system (nephr/o + -ology). Nephrologist treats kidney disease, renal failure, hypertension, manages dialysis.
  • •Neurology: Nervous system (neur/o + -ology). Neurologist treats stroke, epilepsy, MS, Parkinson's, dementia, performs EEG, interprets brain imaging.
  • •Dermatology: Skin (derm/o + -ology). Dermatologist treats skin cancer, eczema, psoriasis, performs biopsies, Mohs surgery.
  • •Orthopedics: Musculoskeletal system (orth/o + ped/o + -ics). Orthopedic surgeon treats fractures, joint replacement, sports injuries, performs arthroscopy.
  • •Urology: Urinary system and male reproductive system (ur/o + -ology). Urologist treats kidney stones, prostate disease, incontinence, performs cystoscopy.
  • •Oncology: Cancer (onc/o + -ology). Medical oncologist (chemotherapy), surgical oncologist (surgery), radiation oncologist (radiation therapy).
  • •Endocrinology: Hormones and endocrine system (endocrin/o + -ology). Endocrinologist treats diabetes, thyroid disease, adrenal disorders, metabolic conditions.
  • •Rheumatology: Joints, connective tissue, autoimmune diseases (rheumat/o + -ology). Rheumatologist treats arthritis, lupus, fibromyalgia, autoimmune conditions.
  • •Hematology: Blood and blood-forming tissues (hemat/o + -ology). Hematologist treats anemia, bleeding disorders, blood cancers, clotting disorders.
  • •Pathology: Disease diagnosis through tissue/fluid analysis (path/o + -ology). Pathologist examines biopsies, autopsies, interprets lab results.
  • •Radiology: Medical imaging (radi/o + -ology). Radiologist interprets X-rays, CT, MRI, ultrasound, performs interventional procedures.
  • •Anesthesiology: Anesthesia and pain management (an- + esthesi/o + -ology). Anesthesiologist administers anesthesia, manages pain, critical care.
  • •Emergency Medicine: Acute care, emergencies. Emergency physician treats trauma, acute illness, performs life-saving procedures.
  • •Internal Medicine: Adult medicine, primary care. Internist manages chronic conditions, coordinates care, preventive medicine.
  • •Pediatrics: Children's medicine (ped/o + -iatrics). Pediatrician treats infants, children, adolescents, developmental issues.
  • •Geriatrics: Elderly medicine (ger/o + -iatrics). Geriatrician manages age-related conditions, polypharmacy, functional decline.
  • •Obstetrics/Gynecology (OB/GYN): Women's reproductive health. Obstetrician (pregnancy, childbirth), Gynecologist (reproductive system, diseases).

Diagnostic and Laboratory Terminology

Diagnostic terminology describes tests, procedures, and findings used to identify diseases and conditions. Laboratory terms describe blood, urine, and other body fluid analyses. Understanding these terms is essential for interpreting test results and understanding diagnostic processes.

Common Diagnostic Procedures:

  • •Biopsy: Tissue sample removal for microscopic examination (bio- + -opsy). Types: needle biopsy, excisional biopsy, punch biopsy, core biopsy. Used to diagnose cancer, infections, inflammatory conditions.
  • •Endoscopy: Internal examination using flexible scope (end/o + -scopy). Upper endoscopy (EGD: esophagogastroduodenoscopy), colonoscopy, bronchoscopy, cystoscopy. Allows visualization, biopsy, and some treatments.
  • •Imaging Studies: Radiography (X-ray), computed tomography (CT scan), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), ultrasound (sonography), nuclear medicine scans (PET, bone scan). Each provides different information about body structures.
  • •Electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG): Heart electrical activity recording (electr/o + cardi/o + -gram). Diagnoses arrhythmias, heart attacks, heart block, chamber enlargement.
  • •Electroencephalogram (EEG): Brain electrical activity recording (electr/o + encephal/o + -gram). Diagnoses epilepsy, brain death, sleep disorders, encephalopathies.
  • •Lumbar Puncture (LP, Spinal Tap): CSF collection from lumbar spine (lumb/o + -ar, puncture). Diagnoses meningitis, measures CSF pressure, detects bleeding, analyzes CSF composition.
  • •Arterial Blood Gas (ABG): Blood sample from artery. Measures pH, oxygen, carbon dioxide, bicarbonate. Assesses acid-base balance, respiratory function.

Laboratory Tests and Values:

  • •Complete Blood Count (CBC): Measures red blood cells (RBC), white blood cells (WBC), platelets, hemoglobin (Hgb), hematocrit (Hct). Diagnoses anemia, infection, bleeding disorders.
  • •Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP): Measures glucose, electrolytes (Na, K, Cl, CO₂), kidney function (BUN, creatinine), liver function (AST, ALT, bilirubin, albumin). Assesses metabolic status, organ function.
  • •Lipid Panel: Measures cholesterol (total, LDL, HDL), triglycerides. Assesses cardiovascular risk, guides treatment for hyperlipidemia.
  • •Urinalysis (UA): Examines urine for appearance, pH, specific gravity, protein, glucose, ketones, blood, white blood cells, bacteria, casts, crystals. Diagnoses UTIs, kidney disease, diabetes.
  • •Culture and Sensitivity (C&S): Grows microorganisms from samples (blood, urine, sputum, wound) and tests antibiotic effectiveness. Guides antibiotic selection.
  • •Tumor Markers: Blood tests for substances produced by cancers. Examples: PSA (prostate), CA-125 (ovarian), CEA (colorectal), AFP (liver). Used for screening, monitoring treatment, detecting recurrence.
  • •Coagulation Studies: PT (prothrombin time), INR (international normalized ratio), PTT (partial thromboplastin time), platelet count. Assesses bleeding risk, monitors anticoagulation therapy.

Pathological Findings Terminology:

  • •Benign: Non-cancerous, not malignant. Benign tumors do not invade or metastasize, but may cause problems by size or location.
  • •Malignant: Cancerous, invasive, can metastasize. Malignant tumors invade surrounding tissue and spread to distant sites.
  • •Metastasis: Spread of cancer to distant sites (meta- + stasis). Metastatic disease indicates advanced cancer, requires systemic treatment.
  • •Acute: Sudden onset, short duration. Acute conditions develop quickly and resolve or become chronic.
  • •Chronic: Long duration, persistent. Chronic conditions last months to years, may require ongoing management.
  • •Idiopathic: Unknown cause (idi/o + path/o + -ic). Many conditions are idiopathic despite extensive investigation.
  • •Iatrogenic: Caused by medical treatment (iatr/o + gen/o + -ic). Iatrogenic infections, complications from procedures or medications.
  • •Etiology: Cause of disease (eti/o + -ology). Understanding etiology guides treatment and prevention.
  • •Pathogenesis: Development of disease (path/o + gen/o + -esis). Describes how disease develops from cause to manifestation.
  • •Prognosis: Predicted outcome of disease (pro- + gnosis). Good prognosis (favorable), poor prognosis (unfavorable), guarded prognosis (uncertain).

Clinical Applications and Word Analysis

Medical terminology is applied in clinical settings for documentation, communication, and patient care. Understanding how to analyze and construct medical terms is essential for healthcare professionals. Word analysis involves breaking terms into components and understanding how they combine to create meaning.

Word Analysis Technique:

  • •Step 1 - Identify Suffix: Start from the end. Suffix often indicates procedure, condition, or specialty. Example: "gastritis" → suffix is "-itis" (inflammation).
  • •Step 2 - Identify Root: Core meaning, usually in middle. Example: "gastritis" → root is "gastr" (stomach).
  • •Step 3 - Identify Prefix: Beginning of word, modifies meaning. Example: "hypertension" → prefix is "hyper-" (excessive).
  • •Step 4 - Combine Meanings: Read from suffix backward: condition/procedure + root + prefix. Example: "gastritis" = inflammation of stomach. "Hypertension" = excessive pressure condition.
  • •Complex Terms: Multiple roots require careful analysis. Example: "gastroenteritis" = gastr/o (stomach) + enter/o (intestine) + -itis (inflammation) = inflammation of stomach and intestines.

Common Term Examples - Analysis:

  • •Cardiomyopathy: Cardi/o (heart) + my/o (muscle) + -pathy (disease) = heart muscle disease. Types: dilated, hypertrophic, restrictive.
  • •Hepatomegaly: Hepat/o (liver) + -megaly (enlargement) = enlarged liver. Causes: hepatitis, heart failure, cancer, fatty liver.
  • •Nephrolithiasis: Nephr/o (kidney) + lith (stone) + -iasis (condition) = kidney stones. Can cause renal colic (severe pain), hematuria.
  • •Osteoarthritis: Oste/o (bone) + arthr/o (joint) + -itis (inflammation) = bone and joint inflammation. Degenerative joint disease.
  • •Pneumothorax: Pneum/o (air/lung) + thorax (chest) = air in chest cavity. Causes collapsed lung, requires chest tube.
  • •Cholecystectomy: Cholecyst/o (gallbladder) + -ectomy (removal) = gallbladder removal. Common for gallstones (cholelithiasis).
  • •Appendectomy: Appendic/o (appendix) + -ectomy (removal) = appendix removal. Emergency surgery for appendicitis.
  • •Hysterectomy: Hyster/o (uterus) + -ectomy (removal) = uterus removal. Total (uterus and cervix) or partial (uterus only).
  • •Mastectomy: Mast/o (breast) + -ectomy (removal) = breast removal. Partial, total, or radical (includes lymph nodes).
  • •Colonoscopy: Colon/o (colon) + -scopy (examination) = colon examination. Screening for polyps and cancer, diagnostic for symptoms.
  • •Angioplasty: Angi/o (vessel) + -plasty (repair) = vessel repair. Balloon dilation of narrowed artery, often with stent placement.
  • •Arthroscopy: Arthr/o (joint) + -scopy (examination) = joint examination. Minimally invasive, used for diagnosis and treatment.

Documentation and Communication:

  • •SOAP Notes: Structured documentation format. S (Subjective: patient's description), O (Objective: measurable findings), A (Assessment: diagnosis), P (Plan: treatment). Standard format for medical records.
  • •Chief Complaint (CC): Primary reason for visit in patient's words. Example: "CC: chest pain for 2 hours."
  • •History of Present Illness (HPI): Detailed description of current problem. Includes onset, location, quality, severity, timing, context, modifying factors, associated symptoms.
  • •Past Medical History (PMH): Previous illnesses, surgeries, conditions. Chronic diseases, hospitalizations, surgeries listed.
  • •Review of Systems (ROS): Systematic review of body systems. General, cardiovascular, respiratory, gastrointestinal, genitourinary, musculoskeletal, neurological, etc.
  • •Differential Diagnosis: List of possible diagnoses (differ + -ent, diagnos + -is). Narrowed based on history, physical exam, test results.
  • •Clinical Decision Making: Using medical terminology to describe findings, formulate diagnoses, and plan treatment. Precise terminology reduces errors and improves communication.
