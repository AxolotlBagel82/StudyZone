Nutrition: In Depth Analysis

Comprehensive exploration of food science and health

Macronutrients: Comprehensive Analysis

Macronutrients are nutrients required in large quantities that provide energy (calories) and structural components for the body. The three primary macronutrients are carbohydrates, proteins, and fats (lipids). Each serves distinct functions: carbohydrates are the primary energy source, proteins build and repair tissues, and fats provide energy, insulation, and aid in vitamin absorption. Understanding macronutrient metabolism, recommended intakes, and food sources is essential for optimal health and performance.

Carbohydrates: The Primary Energy Source

  • •Simple Carbohydrates (Sugars): Monosaccharides: glucose (primary energy source for cells, transported in blood, stored as glycogen in liver and muscles), fructose (fruit sugar, sweeter than glucose, metabolized in liver, found in fruits, honey, high-fructose corn syrup), galactose (component of lactose, converted to glucose in liver). Disaccharides: sucrose (glucose + fructose, table sugar, most common added sugar, broken down by sucrase), lactose (glucose + galactose, milk sugar, broken down by lactase, lactose intolerance results from lactase deficiency), maltose (glucose + glucose, product of starch breakdown, found in malted grains). Simple carbohydrates provide quick energy (4 kcal/gram) but can cause rapid blood glucose spikes and subsequent crashes. Sources: fruits, milk, sweets, processed foods, soft drinks.
  • •Complex Carbohydrates (Polysaccharides): Starch: storage form in plants, amylose (linear chains) and amylopectin (branched chains), found in grains (wheat, rice, corn, oats), legumes (beans, lentils), potatoes, corn. Glycogen: storage form in animals and humans, highly branched, stored in liver (~100g, maintains blood glucose) and muscles (~400g, provides energy during exercise), depleted after ~18 hours of fasting, replenished through carbohydrate intake. Fiber: non-digestible polysaccharides. Soluble fiber (dissolves in water, forms gel, slows digestion, lowers cholesterol by binding bile acids, regulates blood glucose, sources: oats, barley, legumes, apples, citrus fruits, psyllium). Insoluble fiber (does not dissolve, adds bulk, promotes regularity, prevents constipation, sources: whole grains, bran, nuts, seeds, vegetables). Recommended fiber intake: 25-38g daily for adults (14g per 1000 kcal), most Americans consume <15g daily.
  • •Glycemic Index (GI) and Glycemic Load (GL): Glycemic Index: measures how quickly 50g of carbohydrates in a food raises blood glucose (0-100 scale). High GI (>70): rapid spike (white bread, white rice, potatoes, sugar, most breakfast cereals). Medium GI (56-69): moderate spike (whole wheat bread, brown rice, sweet potatoes). Low GI (<55): gradual rise (most fruits, vegetables, legumes, whole grains, nuts). Glycemic Load: accounts for both GI and carbohydrate amount (GI × grams carbohydrates ÷ 100), more practical measure. GL <10 (low), 11-19 (medium), >20 (high). Factors affecting GI: food processing (refined > whole grain), cooking method (longer cooking increases GI), ripeness (riper fruits have higher GI), fat/fiber/protein content (lowers GI), individual variation. Low-GI diets may reduce diabetes risk, improve satiety, support weight management, provide sustained energy.
  • •Carbohydrate Metabolism: Digestion: begins in mouth (salivary amylase), continues in small intestine (pancreatic amylase, brush border enzymes: maltase, sucrase, lactase). Absorption: glucose and galactose via active transport (SGLT1), fructose via facilitated diffusion (GLUT5), enter bloodstream, transported to liver via portal vein. Glucose regulation: insulin (released by pancreas when blood glucose high, promotes glucose uptake by cells, glycogen synthesis, fat storage, protein synthesis), glucagon (released when blood glucose low, promotes glycogen breakdown, gluconeogenesis, fat breakdown). Glycogenesis: glucose → glycogen (when energy needs met). Glycogenolysis: glycogen → glucose (during fasting, exercise). Gluconeogenesis: non-carbohydrate sources (amino acids, lactate, glycerol) → glucose (during prolonged fasting, low-carb diets).
  • •Recommended Carbohydrate Intake: 45-65% of total calories (Dietary Guidelines for Americans). Minimum: 130g/day for brain function (glucose requirement). For 2000 kcal diet: 225-325g carbohydrates (900-1300 kcal). Active individuals: higher needs (6-10g/kg body weight for endurance athletes, glycogen replenishment crucial post-exercise). Low-carb diets: <130g/day (ketogenic <50g/day), may cause ketosis (fat breakdown produces ketones for energy), controversy regarding long-term health effects. Quality matters: emphasize whole grains, fruits, vegetables, legumes over refined grains, added sugars.
  • •Health Implications: Excess refined carbohydrates: linked to obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome. Added sugars: American Heart Association recommends <25g/day (women), <36g/day (men), average American consumes ~77g/day. Adequate fiber: reduces risk of heart disease, type 2 diabetes, colorectal cancer, promotes digestive health, supports healthy gut microbiome. Carbohydrate timing: pre-exercise (1-4 hours before, replenish glycogen), during exercise (>1 hour, maintain blood glucose, 30-60g/hour), post-exercise (within 30 minutes, 1-1.5g/kg body weight, optimal glycogen replenishment window).

Proteins: Building Blocks of Life

  • •Amino Acids: Building blocks of proteins. 20 standard amino acids. Essential amino acids (9, cannot be synthesized, must be obtained from diet): histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, valine. Non-essential amino acids (11, can be synthesized by body): alanine, arginine, asparagine, aspartic acid, cysteine, glutamic acid, glutamine, glycine, proline, serine, tyrosine. Conditionally essential: normally non-essential but become essential during illness, stress, or growth (arginine, cysteine, glutamine, tyrosine). Branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs): leucine, isoleucine, valine, important for muscle protein synthesis, taken as supplements by athletes. Structure: central carbon, amino group (NH₂), carboxyl group (COOH), hydrogen atom, side chain (R-group, determines properties).
  • •Protein Functions: Structural: collagen (skin, bones, tendons, most abundant protein), keratin (hair, nails), actin and myosin (muscle contraction). Enzymatic: all enzymes are proteins, catalyze biochemical reactions (digestion, metabolism, DNA replication). Hormonal: insulin, glucagon, growth hormone, thyroid hormones (some are proteins/peptides). Transport: hemoglobin (oxygen transport), lipoproteins (lipid transport), albumin (nutrient transport, maintains oncotic pressure). Immune function: antibodies (immunoglobulins), complement proteins, cytokines. Fluid balance: albumin maintains osmotic pressure, prevents edema. Acid-base balance: proteins act as buffers, maintain pH. Energy: 4 kcal/gram, used when carbohydrates/fats insufficient (starvation, very low-calorie diets, excessive intake), protein sparing (adequate carbohydrates/fats preserve protein for essential functions).
  • •Protein Quality and Bioavailability: Complete proteins: contain all essential amino acids in adequate amounts (animal proteins: meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy; some plant proteins: quinoa, soy, chia seeds, hemp seeds, buckwheat). Incomplete proteins: lacking or low in one or more essential amino acids (most plant proteins: grains, legumes, nuts, seeds). Complementary proteins: combining incomplete proteins to create complete protein (rice + beans, whole wheat bread + peanut butter, hummus + pita, not necessary at same meal if eaten within 24 hours). Protein quality measures: biological value (BV: proportion of absorbed protein retained, egg protein = 100, reference standard), protein digestibility-corrected amino acid score (PDCAAS: accounts for digestibility, amino acid profile, truncated to 1.0, whey/casein/soy = 1.0, most animal proteins >0.9), digestible indispensable amino acid score (DIAAS: newer method, more accurate for low-quality proteins).
  • •Protein Sources: Animal sources: highest quality, complete proteins, higher bioavailability, contain vitamin B12, heme iron, but also saturated fat, cholesterol (lean sources preferred: skinless poultry, fish, lean cuts of meat, eggs, low-fat dairy). Plant sources: legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas, peas, excellent fiber, folate, iron, zinc, magnesium, no cholesterol, lower saturated fat), nuts and seeds (almonds, walnuts, chia, hemp, pumpkin seeds, healthy fats, vitamin E), whole grains (quinoa, amaranth, teff, bulgur, whole wheat, brown rice, fiber, B vitamins), soy products (tofu, tempeh, edamame, complete protein, isoflavones, controversy regarding health effects). Protein supplements: whey protein (fast-digesting, high leucine, optimal post-workout), casein protein (slow-digesting, sustained amino acid release), plant-based powders (pea, rice, hemp, blends for completeness).
  • •Protein Requirements and Metabolism: Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA): 0.8g/kg body weight for adults (0.36g/lb), for 70kg person = 56g/day. Higher needs: infants (1.5g/kg), children/adolescents (0.95-1.0g/kg during growth), pregnant women (+25g/day), lactating women (+20-25g/day), athletes (1.2-2.0g/kg, endurance: 1.2-1.4g/kg, strength/power: 1.6-2.0g/kg, muscle protein synthesis optimization), elderly (1.0-1.2g/kg, prevent sarcopenia, age-related muscle loss), illness/stress (1.2-2.0g/kg, increased protein turnover, wound healing). Digestion: begins in stomach (pepsin, HCl denatures proteins), continues in small intestine (trypsin, chymotrypsin, pancreatic enzymes, brush border peptidases), absorbed as amino acids/dipeptides/tripeptides. Protein turnover: constant synthesis and breakdown, amino acid pool maintained through dietary intake and breakdown of body proteins, nitrogen balance (positive: growth, pregnancy, muscle gain; negative: starvation, illness, muscle loss; equilibrium: healthy adults).
  • •Protein Timing and Distribution: Post-exercise: 20-40g high-quality protein within 2 hours, stimulates muscle protein synthesis, enhances recovery (anabolic window, may extend to 24 hours for optimal recovery). Per-meal distribution: 20-30g per meal (3-4 meals/day) may optimize muscle protein synthesis better than consuming most protein at one meal, especially for athletes. Pre-sleep protein: 30-40g casein protein may enhance overnight muscle protein synthesis, prevent muscle breakdown. Meal timing: regular protein intake throughout day supports satiety, metabolic rate, muscle mass maintenance.

Fats (Lipids): Essential for Health

  • •Fatty Acid Structure and Classification: Basic structure: carboxyl group (COOH), hydrocarbon chain (4-24 carbons), methyl group (CH₃). Saturated fatty acids: no double bonds, solid at room temperature, all carbons "saturated" with hydrogens. Sources: animal fats (meat, dairy, butter, lard), tropical oils (coconut oil, palm oil), processed foods. Types: short-chain (4-6 carbons, butter, fermented foods), medium-chain (8-12 carbons, coconut oil, MCT oil, quickly metabolized), long-chain (14+ carbons, most dietary fats). Health effects: raise LDL cholesterol, may increase heart disease risk, but recent research suggests complex (lauric, myristic, palmitic, stearic acids have different effects). Recommendations: <10% of calories from saturated fat (American Heart Association), <7% for cardiovascular health.
  • •Unsaturated Fatty Acids: One or more double bonds in carbon chain, liquid at room temperature, "healthy fats." Monounsaturated fatty acids (MUFAs): one double bond, sources: olive oil, canola oil, avocados, nuts (almonds, peanuts, cashews), seeds, olives. Benefits: improve cholesterol profile (lower LDL, raise HDL), reduce inflammation, may reduce heart disease risk, Mediterranean diet staple. Polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs): two or more double bonds. Omega-3 fatty acids: alpha-linolenic acid (ALA, essential, plant sources: flaxseeds, chia seeds, walnuts, canola oil, converted to EPA/DHA in limited amounts), eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA, fish oil, anti-inflammatory, cardiovascular health, mood), docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, fish oil, brain development, cognitive function, vision, recommended during pregnancy/lactation). Omega-6 fatty acids: linoleic acid (LA, essential, vegetable oils: corn, soybean, sunflower), arachidonic acid (AA, can be synthesized, found in animal products, pro-inflammatory in excess). Omega-3:omega-6 ratio: optimal 1:1 to 1:4, modern Western diet often 1:15-1:20 (excessive omega-6), imbalance promotes inflammation.
  • •Trans Fats: Unsaturated fats with trans double bond configuration (hydrogen atoms on opposite sides). Naturally occurring (small amounts in dairy, meat, from bacterial fermentation in ruminants, conjugated linoleic acid CLA may have benefits). Artificially created (hydrogenation: adding hydrogens to liquid oils, creates solid fats, extends shelf life, improves texture in processed foods, partially hydrogenated oils contain trans fats, fully hydrogenated oils do not). Health effects: increase LDL cholesterol, decrease HDL cholesterol, increase triglycerides, promote inflammation, increase heart disease risk (worse than saturated fats), linked to type 2 diabetes, cognitive decline. Regulations: FDA banned partially hydrogenated oils in 2018 (removed from "generally recognized as safe" GRAS list), trans fat <0.5g can be listed as 0g on labels (check ingredients for "partially hydrogenated").
  • •Fat Functions and Metabolism: Energy: 9 kcal/gram (most energy-dense macronutrient), stored as triglycerides in adipose tissue, provides energy during rest, low-intensity exercise, prolonged fasting. Fat-soluble vitamin absorption: vitamins A, D, E, K require fat for absorption (dietary fat essential). Cell membranes: phospholipids form lipid bilayer, cholesterol provides structural integrity, fluidity. Organ protection: cushions organs (visceral fat), insulates body (subcutaneous fat), maintains body temperature. Hormone production: cholesterol precursor for steroid hormones (cortisol, aldosterone, estrogen, testosterone, vitamin D), eicosanoids (prostaglandins, leukotrienes, thromboxanes from omega-3/omega-6). Digestion: begins in mouth (lingual lipase), continues in small intestine (pancreatic lipase, bile emulsifies fats), absorbed via lymphatic system (chylomicrons) → bloodstream. Transport: chylomicrons (dietary fats), VLDL (very-low-density lipoprotein, transport triglycerides), LDL (low-density lipoprotein, "bad cholesterol," delivers cholesterol to cells), HDL (high-density lipoprotein, "good cholesterol," removes cholesterol from cells, returns to liver).
  • •Recommended Fat Intake: 20-35% of total calories (Dietary Guidelines for Americans). For 2000 kcal diet: 44-78g fat (400-700 kcal). Minimum: 20% to ensure adequate fat-soluble vitamin absorption, essential fatty acid intake. Saturated fat: <10% of calories (<200 kcal from saturated fat in 2000 kcal diet, <22g), <7% for cardiovascular health. Trans fat: minimize, ideally 0g. Monounsaturated fat: 10-15% of calories (olive oil, avocados, nuts). Polyunsaturated fat: 8-10% of calories, omega-3: 1.1-1.6g/day (women), 1.6g/day (men), EPA+DHA: 250-500mg/day for cardiovascular health, higher during pregnancy (300mg DHA/day). Cholesterol: dietary cholesterol less important than saturated/trans fat (body synthesizes cholesterol, saturated fat raises blood cholesterol more than dietary cholesterol), <300mg/day (general), <200mg/day (heart disease risk).
  • •Health Implications: Healthy fats: Mediterranean diet (high in MUFAs, omega-3s, associated with reduced heart disease, improved cognitive function, longevity), DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension, includes healthy fats), may reduce inflammation, improve insulin sensitivity, support brain health. Excessive fat intake: obesity (fat is calorie-dense), increased heart disease risk (especially saturated/trans fats), may contribute to metabolic syndrome. Low-fat diets: may reduce fat-soluble vitamin absorption, essential fatty acid deficiency, may not be superior to moderate-fat diets for weight loss, quality of fats matters more than quantity. Ketogenic diet: very high fat (70-80% calories), very low carbohydrate (<50g/day), induces ketosis (fat breakdown produces ketones), used for epilepsy, weight loss, type 2 diabetes management, controversy regarding long-term safety and sustainability.

Micronutrients: Vitamins and Minerals

Micronutrients are nutrients required in small quantities but essential for optimal health. Vitamins are organic compounds that cannot be synthesized in sufficient quantities by the body and must be obtained from diet. Minerals are inorganic elements that serve structural, regulatory, and catalytic functions. Both vitamins and minerals act as cofactors for enzymes, support immune function, regulate metabolism, and prevent deficiency diseases. Deficiencies can cause serious health problems, while excesses (especially of fat-soluble vitamins and some minerals) can be toxic.

Water-Soluble Vitamins:

  • •B-Complex Vitamins: Thiamine (B1): coenzyme for carbohydrate metabolism, energy production, nerve function, deficiency causes beriberi (peripheral neuropathy, heart failure, Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome in alcoholics), RDA: 1.1-1.2mg/day, sources: whole grains, pork, legumes, nuts. Riboflavin (B2): coenzyme for energy metabolism (FAD), antioxidant (glutathione reductase), skin health, deficiency causes ariboflavinosis (glossitis, angular cheilitis, seborrheic dermatitis), RDA: 1.1-1.3mg/day, sources: dairy, eggs, lean meats, green vegetables, fortified cereals. Niacin (B3): coenzyme for energy metabolism (NAD/NADP), cholesterol synthesis, DNA repair, deficiency causes pellagra (dermatitis, dementia, diarrhea, death "4 D's"), high doses (2-6g) lower cholesterol but cause flushing, RDA: 14-16mg/day, sources: meat, fish, poultry, whole grains, legumes, tryptophan (amino acid) can be converted to niacin. Pantothenic Acid (B5): component of coenzyme A (energy metabolism, fatty acid synthesis), RDA: 5mg/day, sources: widespread in foods, deficiency rare. Pyridoxine (B6): coenzyme for amino acid metabolism, neurotransmitter synthesis (serotonin, dopamine, GABA), hemoglobin synthesis, immune function, deficiency causes anemia, neurological symptoms, RDA: 1.3-1.7mg/day, sources: meat, fish, poultry, potatoes, bananas, fortified cereals, high doses (>200mg/day) may cause peripheral neuropathy. Biotin (B7): coenzyme for carboxylation reactions (fatty acid synthesis, gluconeogenesis), hair/skin/nail health (supplement claims), RDA: 30μg/day, sources: eggs (raw egg whites contain avidin, binds biotin, prevents absorption), liver, nuts, legumes. Folate (B9): DNA synthesis, cell division, red blood cell formation, neural tube development (critical during early pregnancy), deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia, neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly), hyperhomocysteinemia (increased heart disease risk), RDA: 400μg/day (600μg pregnant, 500μg lactating), sources: leafy greens, legumes, fortified grains, citrus fruits, folic acid (synthetic form, more stable, better bioavailability, used in supplements/fortification). Cobalamin (B12): DNA synthesis, red blood cell formation, nerve function (myelin sheath maintenance), requires intrinsic factor (stomach protein) for absorption, deficiency causes pernicious anemia (megaloblastic anemia, neurological symptoms, dementia), common in elderly, vegans, absorption disorders, RDA: 2.4μg/day, sources: animal products only (meat, fish, poultry, eggs, dairy), fortified foods, supplements (sublingual, injections for absorption disorders).
  • •Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid): Functions: antioxidant (scavenges free radicals, regenerates vitamin E), collagen synthesis (hydroxylation of proline, lysine, structural protein in skin, bones, blood vessels, connective tissue), immune function (white blood cell function, interferon production, antioxidant protection), iron absorption (reduces ferric Fe³⁺ to ferrous Fe²⁺, enhances non-heme iron absorption), neurotransmitter synthesis (norepinephrine, serotonin), carnitine synthesis (fatty acid transport). Deficiency: scurvy (weakness, fatigue, bleeding gums, poor wound healing, joint pain, anemia, death if untreated, historically common in sailors, rare now). RDA: 75-90mg/day (women), 90mg/day (men), higher for smokers (+35mg), upper limit: 2000mg/day. Sources: citrus fruits (oranges, grapefruits, lemons), berries (strawberries, blueberries), kiwi, bell peppers, broccoli, tomatoes, potatoes. Toxicity: rare, excess excreted in urine, very high doses (>2000mg/day) may cause diarrhea, kidney stones (oxalate formation), interfere with copper absorption. Vitamin C supplements: controversial for common cold prevention (modest reduction in duration, not prevention), high doses not proven to prevent chronic diseases, dietary sources preferable.
  • •Characteristics of Water-Soluble Vitamins: Dissolve in water, not stored in body (except B12, stored in liver), excess excreted in urine, require regular dietary intake, relatively low toxicity risk (except B6, high doses cause neuropathy), cooking can destroy (especially B vitamins, vitamin C, minimize cooking time, avoid excessive water), water-soluble during food preparation, can be lost if cooking water discarded. Absorption: passive diffusion (vitamin C, most B vitamins) or active transport (B12, folate), generally well-absorbed, factors affecting absorption (medications, alcohol, food preparation, age).

Fat-Soluble Vitamins:

  • •Vitamin A: Forms: retinol (animal sources, preformed vitamin A), beta-carotene (plant sources, provitamin A, converted to retinol in body, 6μg beta-carotene = 1μg retinol), other carotenoids (lutein, lycopene, no vitamin A activity but have health benefits). Functions: vision (rhodopsin formation in retina, dark adaptation, prevents night blindness, xerophthalmia, blindness), immune function (maintains epithelial tissues, mucus production, white blood cell function), cell growth and differentiation (gene expression), reproduction (sperm production, fetal development), skin health (maintains epithelial integrity, prevents keratinization). Deficiency: night blindness (early sign), xerophthalmia (dry eyes, Bitot's spots, corneal ulceration, blindness), impaired immune function (increased infection risk, measles mortality), poor growth, skin changes (follicular hyperkeratosis, dry skin). Toxicity (hypervitaminosis A): acute (nausea, vomiting, headache, blurred vision, liver damage, >300,000 IU), chronic (bone pain, hair loss, liver damage, birth defects in pregnancy, >25,000 IU/day for months). RDA: 700-900μg retinol activity equivalents (RAE)/day (women), 900μg/day (men), upper limit: 3000μg/day. Sources: retinol (liver, fish oil, eggs, dairy), beta-carotene (sweet potatoes, carrots, spinach, kale, cantaloupe, apricots).
  • •Vitamin D: "Sunshine vitamin," functions more like hormone. Forms: D2 (ergocalciferol, plant/fortified foods), D3 (cholecalciferol, animal sources, sunlight synthesis in skin, more effective at raising blood levels). Synthesis: UVB radiation (sunlight, 7-dehydrocholesterol in skin → previtamin D3 → vitamin D3), factors affecting synthesis (latitude, season, time of day, skin pigmentation, age, sunscreen use, cloud cover), limited synthesis in winter at high latitudes. Functions: calcium absorption (increases intestinal calcium absorption, maintains blood calcium levels), bone health (promotes bone mineralization, prevents rickets in children, osteomalacia in adults, osteoporosis), immune function (modulates immune response, may reduce autoimmune disease risk, reduce infection risk), cell growth (regulates cell proliferation, differentiation), muscle function (maintains muscle strength, prevents falls in elderly), gene expression (hundreds of genes). Deficiency: rickets (children: bone deformities, growth retardation, muscle weakness), osteomalacia (adults: bone pain, muscle weakness, increased fracture risk), osteoporosis (reduced bone density, increased fracture risk), increased infection risk, autoimmune diseases, depression. Risk factors: limited sun exposure (high latitude, winter, indoor lifestyle, covering skin, sunscreen), dark skin (reduced synthesis), elderly (reduced synthesis, reduced intake), obesity (vitamin D sequestered in fat), malabsorption (Crohn's, celiac), kidney/liver disease (impaired activation). RDA: 600-800 IU/day (15-20μg), higher for elderly (800 IU), upper limit: 4000 IU/day (100μg). Sources: fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, tuna, sardines, cod liver oil), fortified foods (milk, orange juice, cereals, some plant milks), egg yolks, mushrooms (UV-exposed), supplements (D3 preferred). Testing: 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] blood test, optimal levels: 30-50 ng/mL (75-125 nmol/L), deficiency: <20 ng/mL, insufficiency: 20-29 ng/mL.
  • •Vitamin E: Alpha-tocopherol (most active form, used for RDA), other tocopherols and tocotrienols. Functions: antioxidant (protects cell membranes from lipid peroxidation, scavenges free radicals, prevents oxidative damage to LDL cholesterol, may reduce heart disease risk), immune function (T-cell function), blood vessel health (prevents platelet aggregation, improves vasodilation), eye health (may reduce age-related macular degeneration risk, cataracts). Deficiency: rare (stored in adipose tissue, widespread in foods), causes hemolytic anemia (red blood cell breakdown), neurological symptoms (peripheral neuropathy, ataxia), occurs in fat malabsorption disorders (cystic fibrosis, abetalipoproteinemia). Toxicity: rare, high doses (>1000 IU/day) may increase bleeding risk (anticoagulant effect), interfere with vitamin K, mortality concerns (meta-analyses inconsistent). RDA: 15mg/day (22.4 IU natural, 33.3 IU synthetic), upper limit: 1000mg/day (1500 IU natural, 1100 IU synthetic). Sources: vegetable oils (wheat germ, sunflower, safflower), nuts (almonds, hazelnuts, peanuts), seeds (sunflower seeds), green leafy vegetables, fortified cereals.
  • •Vitamin K: Forms: K1 (phylloquinone, plant sources, primary dietary form), K2 (menaquinone, synthesized by bacteria in gut, fermented foods, animal products). Functions: blood clotting (carboxylation of clotting factors II, VII, IX, X, protein C, protein S, activates clotting cascade), bone metabolism (carboxylation of osteocalcin, bone mineralization, may reduce fracture risk, osteoporosis), cardiovascular health (prevents arterial calcification, activates matrix Gla protein). Deficiency: rare (widespread in foods, gut bacteria synthesis), causes bleeding (easy bruising, nosebleeds, bleeding gums, heavy periods, hemorrhagic disease of newborn), risk factors (newborns: sterile gut, low transfer across placenta, routine injection at birth), fat malabsorption (bile duct obstruction, celiac disease, cystic fibrosis), broad-spectrum antibiotics (kill gut bacteria), anticoagulants (warfarin, vitamin K antagonists, requires consistent dietary intake). Toxicity: rare, natural forms have low toxicity, synthetic menadione (not used in supplements) can cause hemolytic anemia. RDA: 90-120μg/day (women), 120μg/day (men), AI (Adequate Intake) used, no upper limit established. Sources: K1 (green leafy vegetables: kale, spinach, broccoli, Brussels sprouts, lettuce, green beans, vegetable oils, fruits), K2 (fermented foods: natto, sauerkraut, cheese, meat, eggs, gut bacteria synthesis).
  • •Characteristics of Fat-Soluble Vitamins: Dissolve in fat, require dietary fat for absorption (fat malabsorption reduces absorption), stored in body (liver, adipose tissue), can accumulate to toxic levels (especially vitamins A and D), less frequent dietary intake needed (can survive periods of deficiency), excretion: slow (via bile, feces), cooking less destructive (more stable than water-soluble), can be lost in cooking fat if discarded. Absorption: requires bile (emulsification), pancreatic lipase, chylomicrons (transported via lymphatic system), fat malabsorption conditions reduce absorption (Crohn's disease, celiac disease, cystic fibrosis, pancreatic insufficiency, bile duct obstruction).

Major Minerals (Macrominerals):

  • •Calcium: Most abundant mineral in body (99% in bones/teeth, 1% in blood/soft tissues). Functions: bone and tooth structure (hydroxyapatite crystals, provides strength, 99% of body calcium), muscle contraction (smooth, skeletal, cardiac muscle, triggers contraction, relaxation), nerve function (neurotransmitter release, nerve impulse transmission), blood clotting (clotting cascade activation), enzyme function (cofactor for many enzymes), hormone secretion (insulin, parathyroid hormone). Regulation: parathyroid hormone (PTH, raises blood calcium: increases bone resorption, kidney reabsorption, intestinal absorption via vitamin D), calcitonin (lowers blood calcium: decreases bone resorption), vitamin D (increases intestinal absorption, kidney reabsorption). Deficiency: rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults), osteoporosis (reduced bone density, increased fracture risk, especially postmenopausal women, elderly), hypocalcemia (low blood calcium: muscle cramps, tetany, seizures, cardiac arrhythmias). RDA: 1000-1300mg/day (varies by age/sex, highest: adolescents 1300mg, pregnant/lactating 1000-1300mg), upper limit: 2500mg/day (may cause kidney stones, hypercalcemia). Sources: dairy (milk, yogurt, cheese: highest bioavailability), fortified foods (orange juice, plant milks, cereals), green leafy vegetables (broccoli, kale, bok choy, spinach: oxalates reduce absorption), fish with bones (sardines, salmon), tofu (calcium-set), almonds, sesame seeds. Absorption: enhanced by vitamin D, acidic environment (gastric acid), lactose (in infants), reduced by oxalates (spinach, rhubarb), phytates (whole grains, legumes), excessive fiber, tannins (tea), excessive protein/sodium (increases urinary calcium loss).
  • •Phosphorus: Second most abundant mineral (85% in bones/teeth with calcium). Functions: bone structure (with calcium), energy metabolism (ATP, ADP, AMP: energy currency of cells), DNA/RNA structure (phosphate backbone), cell membranes (phospholipids), acid-base balance (phosphate buffer), enzyme function (phosphorylation, activation). Deficiency: rare (widespread in foods), causes bone pain, weakness, rickets, osteomalacia. RDA: 700mg/day (adults), upper limit: 4000mg/day (high levels may interfere with calcium absorption, bone health). Sources: protein-rich foods (meat, poultry, fish, dairy, eggs, legumes), whole grains, nuts, seeds, processed foods (food additives: phosphoric acid in sodas, preservatives).
  • •Magnesium: Functions: enzyme function (over 300 enzyme reactions, ATP production, DNA/RNA synthesis, protein synthesis), muscle and nerve function (relaxes muscles, prevents spasms, nerve impulse transmission, neurotransmitter release), bone health (bone structure, density), energy metabolism (glycolysis, Krebs cycle, ATP synthesis), blood pressure regulation (vasodilation), blood glucose control (insulin sensitivity), cardiovascular health (heart rhythm, prevents arrhythmias). Deficiency: common (especially elderly, diabetes, gastrointestinal diseases, alcohol abuse, diuretics), causes muscle cramps, weakness, fatigue, irritability, insomnia, arrhythmias, increased cardiovascular disease risk. RDA: 310-420mg/day (women), 400-420mg/day (men), upper limit: 350mg/day from supplements (diarrhea, nausea). Sources: green leafy vegetables (spinach, Swiss chard), whole grains (brown rice, quinoa, whole wheat), legumes (black beans, chickpeas, lentils), nuts (almonds, cashews, peanuts), seeds (pumpkin, sunflower), dark chocolate, fish (salmon, mackerel), bananas, avocados. Absorption: enhanced by vitamin D, protein, reduced by phytates, oxalates, excessive fiber, calcium (competes for absorption).
  • •Sodium: Major extracellular cation. Functions: fluid balance (osmotic pressure, maintains blood volume, blood pressure), nerve function (action potential, nerve impulse transmission), muscle function (muscle contraction), nutrient absorption (glucose, amino acids via co-transport). Deficiency (hyponatremia): rare with normal diet, causes nausea, headache, confusion, seizures, coma, occurs in excessive sweating, diarrhea, vomiting, kidney disease, heart failure. Excess (hypernatremia): high blood pressure (hypertension: major risk factor for heart disease, stroke, kidney disease), fluid retention (edema), increased cardiovascular disease risk. AI (Adequate Intake): 1500mg/day (minimum), upper limit: 2300mg/day (general population), 1500mg/day (hypertension, African Americans, middle-aged/elderly), average American consumes 3400mg/day (excess). Sources: table salt (sodium chloride, 40% sodium: 1 teaspoon = 2300mg sodium), processed foods (75% of dietary sodium: bread, processed meats, canned foods, soups, sauces, snacks, fast food, restaurant meals), natural sources (meat, dairy, vegetables: small amounts). Reduction strategies: read labels, choose fresh foods, cook at home, use herbs/spices instead of salt, rinse canned foods, limit processed foods, restaurant awareness.
  • •Potassium: Major intracellular cation. Functions: fluid balance (intracellular osmotic pressure), nerve function (action potential, nerve impulse transmission), muscle function (muscle contraction, heart rhythm), blood pressure regulation (reduces blood pressure, counteracts sodium effects, vasodilation, reduces stroke risk), acid-base balance (buffer, maintains pH). Deficiency (hypokalemia): causes muscle weakness, fatigue, cramps, arrhythmias, increased blood pressure, increased stroke risk, occurs in diarrhea, vomiting, diuretics, excessive sweating, kidney disease, inadequate intake. AI: 2600mg/day (women), 3400mg/day (men), most Americans consume <2600mg/day (inadequate). Upper limit: not established (excess rare with food, high-dose supplements may cause hyperkalemia: arrhythmias, cardiac arrest, especially kidney disease). Sources: fruits (bananas, oranges, cantaloupe, apricots, raisins, dates), vegetables (potatoes, sweet potatoes, tomatoes, spinach, broccoli, beans), legumes, dairy, fish, nuts, seeds. DASH diet emphasizes potassium-rich foods for blood pressure control.
  • •Chloride: Major extracellular anion. Functions: fluid balance (osmotic pressure, maintains blood volume), digestion (hydrochloric acid HCl in stomach, protein digestion, kills bacteria), acid-base balance (buffer), nerve function. Deficiency: rare, causes alkalosis, muscle cramps. AI: 2300mg/day, upper limit: 3600mg/day. Sources: table salt (sodium chloride, 60% chloride: 1 teaspoon = 3500mg chloride), processed foods, natural sources.
  • •Sulfur: Component of amino acids (methionine, cysteine), proteins, vitamins (biotin, thiamine), connective tissue (chondroitin sulfate), detoxification (glutathione). Deficiency: rare (adequate protein intake provides sufficient sulfur). No RDA established. Sources: protein-rich foods (meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, nuts).

Trace Minerals (Microminerals):

  • •Iron: Functions: oxygen transport (hemoglobin in red blood cells, carries oxygen to tissues, myoglobin in muscles stores oxygen), energy metabolism (cytochromes in electron transport chain, ATP production), immune function (white blood cell function, antibody production), cognitive function (neurotransmitter synthesis, brain development, children at risk of deficiency). Forms: heme iron (animal sources, Fe²⁺, ferrous, higher bioavailability 15-35%, not affected by inhibitors/enhancers), non-heme iron (plant sources, Fe³⁺, ferric, lower bioavailability 2-20%, affected by enhancers/inhibitors). Absorption: enhanced by vitamin C (reduces Fe³⁺ to Fe²⁺, chelates iron, increases absorption 3-6 fold, consume with plant sources), meat/fish/poultry (MFP factor, enhances non-heme iron absorption), acidic environment (gastric acid). Reduced by: phytates (whole grains, legumes, reduces absorption), polyphenols (tannins in tea, coffee, reduces absorption, avoid with meals), calcium (competes for absorption, reduces both heme and non-heme), oxalates (spinach, reduces non-heme), antacids (reduce gastric acid, reduce absorption). Storage: ferritin (storage form, liver, spleen, bone marrow, measured in blood to assess iron stores), transferrin (transport protein, carries iron in blood), total iron-binding capacity (TIBC, measures transferrin capacity). Deficiency (iron deficiency anemia): most common nutritional deficiency worldwide, causes fatigue, weakness, pallor, shortness of breath, poor concentration, impaired immune function, pica (craving non-food items: ice, dirt, clay), increased infection risk, poor cognitive development (children), poor pregnancy outcomes. Risk groups: women (menstrual blood loss, pregnancy), infants/children (growth, limited diet), adolescents (growth, poor diet), vegetarians/vegans (plant sources less absorbable), elderly (reduced intake, absorption, blood loss), chronic blood loss (gastrointestinal bleeding, parasites). RDA: 8mg/day (men), 18mg/day (women 19-50 years, menstrual losses), 8mg/day (women >50 years), 27mg/day (pregnant), 9-10mg/day (lactating), upper limit: 45mg/day (constipation, nausea, organ damage, hemochromatosis risk). Sources: heme (red meat, poultry, fish, seafood, organ meats: liver highest), non-heme (legumes: beans, lentils, chickpeas, tofu, spinach, fortified cereals, whole grains, nuts, seeds, dried fruits). Iron supplementation: ferrous sulfate (most common, best absorbed, may cause constipation, nausea, take with food, vitamin C enhances absorption, avoid with tea/coffee, calcium supplements).
  • •Zinc: Functions: enzyme function (over 300 enzymes, DNA/RNA synthesis, protein synthesis, cell division), immune function (white blood cell function, antibody production, wound healing, reduces infection duration), growth and development (cell division, protein synthesis, essential during childhood, adolescence, pregnancy), wound healing (cell division, protein synthesis, collagen formation), taste and smell (taste buds, olfactory receptors, deficiency causes loss of taste/smell), antioxidant (superoxide dismutase SOD), gene expression (zinc fingers, DNA-binding proteins). Deficiency: causes growth retardation (children), delayed sexual maturation, impaired immune function (increased infection risk, poor wound healing), loss of taste/smell, skin lesions (acrodermatitis enteropathica, genetic disorder), hair loss, diarrhea, cognitive impairment. Risk groups: vegetarians/vegans (plant sources less absorbable, phytates), elderly (reduced intake, absorption), chronic diarrhea, malabsorption (Crohn's, celiac), alcohol abuse, sickle cell disease. RDA: 8-11mg/day (women), 11mg/day (men), upper limit: 40mg/day (may cause nausea, vomiting, impaired copper absorption, immune suppression). Sources: oysters (highest), red meat, poultry, seafood, dairy, eggs, legumes, whole grains, nuts, seeds, fortified cereals. Absorption: enhanced by protein (amino acids, especially histidine, cysteine), reduced by phytates (whole grains, legumes, significantly reduces absorption, soaking/sprouting/fermenting reduces phytates), calcium (competes), iron (high doses compete), fiber (binds zinc). Zinc supplements: may reduce common cold duration (zinc lozenges, early treatment, controversy), wound healing (topical), acne (oral, controversy).
  • •Iodine: Functions: thyroid hormone synthesis (thyroxine T4, triiodothyronine T3, regulates metabolism, growth, development, brain development during pregnancy/infancy). Deficiency: causes goiter (thyroid enlargement, compensation for low iodine), hypothyroidism (fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, depression, cognitive impairment), cretinism (severe mental retardation, stunted growth, congenital hypothyroidism in infants, irreversible if not treated early), pregnancy complications (miscarriage, stillbirth, congenital anomalies, mental retardation). Risk areas: regions with iodine-deficient soil (mountainous areas, inland regions, prior to iodized salt: parts of Africa, Asia, Europe). RDA: 150μg/day (adults), 220μg/day (pregnant), 290μg/day (lactating), upper limit: 1100μg/day (may cause hyperthyroidism, goiter). Sources: iodized salt (primary source in US, 1 teaspoon = 400μg), seafood (fish, shellfish, seaweed: highest), dairy (iodine in feed, cleaning solutions), eggs, some bread (iodate dough conditioners). Iodized salt: public health success, introduced in 1920s, virtually eliminated iodine deficiency in US.
  • •Selenium: Functions: antioxidant (glutathione peroxidase, protects cells from oxidative damage, works with vitamin E), thyroid function (converts T4 to active T3), immune function, reproduction. Deficiency: rare (widespread in foods, varies by soil selenium content), causes Keshan disease (cardiomyopathy, endemic in China: low selenium soil), Kashin-Beck disease (osteoarthritis, endemic in China, Russia), impaired immune function, hypothyroidism. Toxicity: selenosis (hair loss, nail brittleness, garlic breath, neurological symptoms, occurs at >900μg/day). RDA: 55μg/day, upper limit: 400μg/day. Sources: Brazil nuts (highest, very high: limit to 1-2 per day), seafood, meat, poultry, eggs, dairy, whole grains (varies by soil), mushrooms.
  • •Copper: Functions: enzyme function (cytochrome c oxidase: energy production, superoxide dismutase: antioxidant, tyrosinase: melanin synthesis, lysyl oxidase: collagen cross-linking), iron metabolism (ceruloplasmin: ferroxidase, converts Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ for transport, iron absorption), connective tissue (collagen, elastin formation), nerve function (myelin sheath maintenance), immune function, antioxidant. Deficiency: rare (widespread in foods), causes anemia (iron deficiency-like, but iron supplements don't help), neutropenia (low white blood cells), bone abnormalities, neurological symptoms (rare: Menkes disease, genetic disorder, copper transport defect). Toxicity: rare (acute: vomiting, diarrhea, liver damage, chronic: Wilson's disease, genetic disorder, copper accumulation, liver damage, neurological symptoms, treatment: chelation, zinc supplements). RDA: 900μg/day, upper limit: 10,000μg/day (10mg). Sources: organ meats (liver), seafood (oysters, shellfish), nuts (cashews, almonds), seeds, whole grains, legumes, dark chocolate, potatoes.
  • •Manganese: Functions: enzyme function (superoxide dismutase: antioxidant, pyruvate carboxylase: gluconeogenesis, arginase: urea cycle), bone formation, wound healing, metabolism (carbohydrate, amino acid, cholesterol metabolism). Deficiency: rare (widespread in foods), causes bone abnormalities, impaired growth, skin changes. Toxicity: rare (inhalation: manganism, neurological symptoms, Parkinson's-like, occupational exposure), dietary excess unlikely. AI: 1.8-2.3mg/day (women), 2.3mg/day (men), upper limit: 11mg/day. Sources: whole grains, nuts, seeds, legumes, leafy vegetables, tea, pineapple.
  • •Chromium: Functions: glucose metabolism (enhances insulin action, improves glucose tolerance, insulin sensitivity), lipid metabolism (may improve cholesterol profile). Deficiency: rare, causes impaired glucose tolerance (insulin resistance), elevated blood glucose. Chromium supplements: controversial for diabetes, weight loss, muscle gain (evidence weak, not recommended by major organizations). AI: 20-25μg/day (women), 30-35μg/day (men), upper limit: not established. Sources: whole grains, broccoli, green beans, nuts, brewer's yeast, meat, cheese.
  • •Fluoride: Functions: dental health (strengthens tooth enamel, prevents cavities, remineralization, reduces acid production by bacteria), bone health (may increase bone density, reduces fracture risk, controversial). Deficiency: increased dental caries (cavities), weaker tooth enamel. Toxicity: fluorosis (mottled tooth enamel, white/brown spots, occurs during tooth development, from excessive fluoride intake, water fluoridation safe). AI: 3-4mg/day (women), 4mg/day (men), upper limit: 10mg/day. Sources: fluoridated water (primary source in US, optimal: 0.7-1.2 ppm, added to ~75% of US water supply, major public health success, reduces cavities by 25-40%), tea (high), seafood, some foods (varies by water content), toothpaste (topical benefit, but children may swallow, use pea-sized amount, supervise, avoid if fluoridated water). Water fluoridation: safe, effective, cost-effective, recommended by major health organizations (CDC, ADA, WHO, AAFP).

Digestion and Metabolism

Digestion is the mechanical and chemical breakdown of food into smaller, absorbable molecules. It occurs in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract through the coordinated action of digestive organs, enzymes, and hormones. Absorption is the process by which nutrients pass from the GI tract into the bloodstream or lymphatic system. Metabolism encompasses all biochemical processes that convert nutrients into energy (catabolism) and building blocks for growth and repair (anabolism). Understanding digestion and metabolism is essential for optimizing nutrient absorption and energy utilization.

The Digestive System: Comprehensive Overview

  • •Mouth (Oral Cavity): Mechanical digestion: chewing (mastication) breaks food into smaller pieces, increases surface area for enzyme action, mixes food with saliva, forms bolus (soft mass). Chemical digestion: salivary amylase (ptyalin) begins carbohydrate digestion (starch → maltose, dextrins, inactivated by stomach acid), lingual lipase (minimal fat digestion, more important in infants), lysozyme (antibacterial enzyme, protects oral health). Saliva: 99% water, enzymes (amylase, lipase), mucin (lubrication, forms bolus), electrolytes, antimicrobial compounds (lysozyme, lactoferrin, antibodies). Functions: lubrication (aids swallowing), taste (dissolves food, stimulates taste receptors), digestion (amylase, lipase), protection (neutralizes acids, antimicrobial), speech (moistens mouth, tongue movement). Swallowing: voluntary phase (tongue pushes bolus to pharynx), involuntary phase (pharyngeal and esophageal phases, coordinated by brainstem, peristalsis: wave-like muscle contractions).
  • •Esophagus: Muscular tube connecting pharynx to stomach (~25cm long). Peristalsis: coordinated muscle contractions push bolus toward stomach (smooth muscle, involuntary), gravity assists in upright position but not required (works in space, upside down). Lower esophageal sphincter (LES): prevents gastric reflux (heartburn, GERD: gastroesophageal reflux disease, when weakened: hiatal hernia, obesity, pregnancy, certain foods/drinks relax LES).
  • •Stomach: J-shaped muscular sac (capacity: 1-1.5L, can expand to 2-4L). Functions: mechanical digestion (churning, mixing food with gastric juice, forms chyme: semiliquid mixture), chemical digestion (protein digestion begins), storage (holds food, regulates delivery to small intestine), protection (acidic environment kills bacteria, prevents infection), limited absorption (water, alcohol, some medications, aspirin). Gastric secretions: hydrochloric acid (HCl: pH 1-2, denatures proteins, activates pepsinogen → pepsin, kills bacteria, optimal pH for pepsin), pepsinogen → pepsin (protein digestion: proteins → polypeptides, peptides, optimal pH 1.5-2.5), gastric lipase (minimal fat digestion, more important in infants), intrinsic factor (binds vitamin B12, essential for absorption, prevents pernicious anemia), mucus (protects stomach lining from acid, prevents ulcers: peptic ulcer disease when disrupted). Gastric phases: cephalic phase (smell, sight, taste of food, triggers gastric secretion, vagus nerve, 30% of secretion), gastric phase (food in stomach, distension, chemical stimulation, gastrin hormone, 60% of secretion), intestinal phase (chyme enters duodenum, inhibits gastric secretion, hormones: secretin, cholecystokinin CCK, GIP: gastric inhibitory peptide). Stomach emptying: regulated by neural and hormonal signals, typically 2-4 hours, faster for liquids (water: ~20 minutes), carbohydrates (simple: faster, complex: slower), slower for proteins, fats (fatty meals delay emptying, CCK released), emotional state (stress delays, relaxation facilitates).
  • •Small Intestine: Primary site of digestion and absorption (~6-7 meters long, but compact due to folds, villi, microvilli: increases surface area to ~250m², size of tennis court). Regions: duodenum (first 25cm, receives chyme from stomach, pancreatic juice, bile, most active digestion), jejunum (middle 2.5m, most nutrient absorption), ileum (final 3.5m, completes absorption, especially B12, bile acids). Digestive secretions: pancreatic juice (bicarbonate: neutralizes acidic chyme, raises pH to 7-8, optimal for pancreatic enzymes, protects intestinal lining; pancreatic amylase: carbohydrate digestion, starch → maltose, dextrins; pancreatic lipase: fat digestion, triglycerides → fatty acids, monoglycerides, requires bile for emulsification; trypsin, chymotrypsin: protein digestion, polypeptides → peptides, amino acids; carboxypeptidase: peptides → amino acids; ribonuclease, deoxyribonuclease: nucleic acid digestion). Bile: produced by liver (500-1000mL/day), stored in gallbladder (concentrates bile 5-10x), released when fat enters duodenum (CCK stimulates gallbladder contraction), bile salts (emulsify fats: break large fat droplets into small droplets, increases surface area for lipase, micelle formation for absorption), bilirubin (waste product, gives feces color), cholesterol, phospholipids. Intestinal enzymes (brush border enzymes): lactase (lactose → glucose + galactose, lactase deficiency → lactose intolerance), sucrase (sucrose → glucose + fructose), maltase (maltose → glucose), peptidases (peptides → amino acids, dipeptidases, tripeptidases). Absorption mechanisms: active transport (glucose, galactose, amino acids: requires energy, against concentration gradient, co-transport with sodium), facilitated diffusion (fructose: no energy, with concentration gradient), passive diffusion (small lipids, fat-soluble vitamins, water, electrolytes), endocytosis (some proteins, immunoglobulins in infants, antigen sampling). Micelles: tiny lipid droplets (fatty acids, monoglycerides, fat-soluble vitamins, cholesterol) surrounded by bile salts, transport to enterocytes (intestinal cells), diffuse into cells, reform into chylomicrons (larger lipoproteins), enter lymphatic system (lacteals), transported to bloodstream via thoracic duct (bypasses liver initially, unlike other nutrients).
  • •Large Intestine (Colon): Final section (~1.5 meters long). Functions: water absorption (reabsorbs ~1.5L water/day, from ~2L entering, forms solid feces: ~200g, prevents dehydration, diarrhea: inadequate absorption, constipation: excessive absorption), electrolyte absorption (sodium, chloride, potassium, bicarbonate), vitamin production (gut bacteria synthesize vitamin K, biotin, some B vitamins, absorbed by colon), fiber fermentation (bacteria break down fiber, produces short-chain fatty acids SCFAs: butyrate, propionate, acetate, energy source for colonocytes, may reduce colon cancer risk, promote gut health), waste storage (feces stored in rectum, eliminated via defecation). Gut microbiome: ~100 trillion bacteria (10× more than human cells, 300-1000 species, ~3-5 pounds), functions (digestion: fiber, some carbohydrates, proteins; vitamin synthesis: K, B vitamins; immune function: competes with pathogens, stimulates immune system; metabolism: SCFAs, affects weight, metabolism; brain-gut axis: affects mood, behavior, neurotransmitters). Prebiotics: non-digestible fibers that feed beneficial bacteria (inulin, fructooligosaccharides FOS, galactooligosaccharides GOS, sources: onions, garlic, bananas, asparagus, artichokes, whole grains). Probiotics: live beneficial bacteria (supplements, fermented foods: yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, kombucha, may improve gut health, immune function, digestion, but evidence mixed).
  • •Digestive Hormones: Gastrin (stomach: stimulates HCl, pepsinogen secretion, stomach motility, released when food enters stomach, inhibited by low pH), secretin (duodenum: stimulates bicarbonate secretion from pancreas, bile secretion from liver, inhibits gastric acid secretion, released when acidic chyme enters duodenum), cholecystokinin CCK (duodenum: stimulates pancreatic enzyme secretion, gallbladder contraction, bile release, inhibits gastric emptying, released when fat/protein enters duodenum, promotes satiety: feeling of fullness), gastric inhibitory peptide GIP (duodenum: inhibits gastric acid secretion, stimulates insulin release, released when glucose/fat enters duodenum), motilin (small intestine: stimulates intestinal motility, "housekeeping" contractions: migrating motor complex MMC, between meals), peptide YY PYY (ileum, colon: inhibits gastric acid secretion, slows gastric emptying, reduces appetite, released after meals, promotes satiety), glucagon-like peptide-1 GLP-1 (ileum: stimulates insulin release, inhibits glucagon, slows gastric emptying, reduces appetite, released after meals, diabetes medications mimic GLP-1: GLP-1 agonists, weight loss drugs).

Metabolic Pathways: Energy Production and Utilization

  • •Carbohydrate Metabolism - Glycolysis: Breakdown of glucose (6 carbons) to pyruvate (3 carbons). Location: cytoplasm (all cells). Process: 10 enzymatic steps, 2 ATP invested, 4 ATP produced (net: 2 ATP), 2 NADH produced (electron carriers, used in electron transport chain ETC). Fate of pyruvate: aerobic conditions (oxygen present: pyruvate → acetyl-CoA, enters Krebs cycle, complete oxidation, produces ~36-38 ATP total), anaerobic conditions (no oxygen: pyruvate → lactate, lactic acid fermentation, produces 2 ATP total, occurs in muscles during intense exercise, red blood cells lack mitochondria, yeast: pyruvate → ethanol, alcoholic fermentation). Glycogen metabolism: glycogenesis (glucose → glycogen, occurs after meals, when energy needs met, stimulated by insulin, stored in liver and muscles), glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose, occurs during fasting, exercise, stimulated by glucagon, epinephrine, provides glucose for brain, muscles). Gluconeogenesis: synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate sources (amino acids: alanine, glutamine; lactate: from anaerobic glycolysis; glycerol: from triglyceride breakdown), occurs in liver (primarily), kidneys (minor), during prolonged fasting, low-carb diets, critical for maintaining blood glucose for brain (glucose primary fuel, cannot use fatty acids directly).
  • •Carbohydrate Metabolism - Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle, TCA Cycle): Oxidation of acetyl-CoA (from pyruvate, fatty acids, amino acids) to CO₂, production of electron carriers (NADH, FADH₂) for ETC, some ATP directly. Location: mitochondrial matrix. Process: 8 enzymatic steps, acetyl-CoA (2 carbons) combines with oxaloacetate (4 carbons) → citrate (6 carbons) → series of reactions → oxaloacetate regenerated (cycle), 2 CO₂ released, 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 ATP (GTP) per turn, per glucose: 2 turns (from 2 pyruvate), produces 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂, 2 ATP, 4 CO₂ (total: 6 CO₂ from glucose). Electron carriers: NADH, FADH₂ enter electron transport chain, produce majority of ATP (~34 ATP from ETC).
  • •Carbohydrate Metabolism - Electron Transport Chain (ETC) and Oxidative Phosphorylation: Production of ATP from electron carriers (NADH, FADH₂). Location: inner mitochondrial membrane. Process: electron carriers (NADH, FADH₂) donate electrons to ETC (series of protein complexes: I, II, III, IV), electrons passed along chain, energy released pumps protons (H⁺) across membrane (creates proton gradient), protons flow back through ATP synthase (chemiosmosis), ATP synthesized from ADP + Pi, final electron acceptor: oxygen (O₂ → H₂O, water produced). ATP yield: NADH → ~3 ATP (may be 2.5-3, depends on shuttle system), FADH₂ → ~2 ATP (1.5-2), per glucose: ~30-32 ATP from ETC, total: ~36-38 ATP per glucose (includes glycolysis: 2 ATP, Krebs cycle: 2 ATP, ETC: ~32-34 ATP). Efficiency: ~40% (rest released as heat, maintains body temperature).
  • •Fat Metabolism - Beta-Oxidation: Breakdown of fatty acids for energy. Location: mitochondria (primarily), peroxisomes (very long-chain fatty acids). Process: fatty acid (16-18 carbons typical) activated (attached to CoA, uses 2 ATP), transported into mitochondria (carnitine shuttle), series of reactions removes 2 carbons at a time (acetyl-CoA, enters Krebs cycle), repeats until fatty acid completely oxidized. ATP yield: palmitic acid (16 carbons): 8 acetyl-CoA, ~106-129 ATP (varies), more energy-dense than glucose (fat: 9 kcal/g, carbohydrate: 4 kcal/g). Ketogenesis: when glucose limited (fasting, low-carb diets, starvation), acetyl-CoA converted to ketone bodies (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetone), produced in liver, used by brain, heart, muscles as alternative fuel, provides ~60-70% of brain energy during prolonged fasting (brain adapts to ketones, reduces glucose requirement), ketosis (physiological: low-carb diets, fasting, normal), ketoacidosis (pathological: uncontrolled diabetes, dangerous, life-threatening, pH drops, coma, death).
  • •Fat Metabolism - Lipogenesis: Synthesis of fatty acids from acetyl-CoA (when energy intake exceeds needs). Location: cytoplasm (liver, adipose tissue). Process: acetyl-CoA (from glucose, amino acids, alcohol) converted to malonyl-CoA, series of reactions adds 2 carbons at a time, builds fatty acids (typically 16-18 carbons), combines with glycerol to form triglycerides, stored in adipose tissue (energy reserve, insulation, organ protection). Regulation: insulin stimulates lipogenesis (high-carb meals, excess calories), glucagon, epinephrine inhibit (fasting, exercise, promote fat breakdown).
  • •Protein Metabolism - Protein Synthesis: Building proteins from amino acids. Location: ribosomes (cytoplasm, rough ER). Process: DNA transcription (DNA → mRNA, in nucleus), translation (mRNA → protein, at ribosome), amino acids linked by peptide bonds (requires energy: ATP, GTP), protein folding (3D structure, determines function), post-translational modifications (phosphorylation, glycosylation, etc.). Types: structural proteins (collagen, keratin, actin, myosin), functional proteins (enzymes, hormones, antibodies, transport proteins). Requirements: all 20 amino acids (9 essential must be obtained from diet), adequate energy (if insufficient, amino acids used for energy, not protein synthesis), optimal conditions (adequate nutrition, hormones: insulin, growth hormone, testosterone).
  • •Protein Metabolism - Protein Breakdown (Proteolysis): Breakdown of proteins to amino acids. Occurs: constantly (protein turnover: synthesis and breakdown, maintains dynamic equilibrium), increased during: fasting (gluconeogenesis: amino acids → glucose), stress (illness, injury, cortisol increases breakdown), exercise (muscle protein breakdown, then synthesis: net positive with adequate nutrition, timing). Process: proteases break peptide bonds → amino acids, deamination (removes amino group NH₂, forms ammonia NH₃, toxic, converted to urea in liver, excreted by kidneys), carbon skeleton (enters metabolism: glucose via gluconeogenesis, acetyl-CoA, Krebs cycle, energy production). Amino acid pool: balance between intake (diet), synthesis (non-essential), breakdown (body proteins), excretion (urea, other nitrogen compounds), must be positive (intake + synthesis > breakdown) for growth, muscle gain, wound healing.
  • •Metabolic States: Fed state (postprandial: 0-4 hours after meal): high blood glucose, insulin high, promotes: glucose uptake by cells, glycogen synthesis, fat synthesis (if excess), protein synthesis; energy source: glucose (primary), dietary fat stored, dietary protein used for synthesis. Fasting state (4-12 hours): blood glucose drops, insulin decreases, glucagon increases, promotes: glycogen breakdown (liver releases glucose), gluconeogenesis (liver synthesizes glucose from amino acids, lactate, glycerol), fat breakdown begins; energy source: glucose (from glycogen, gluconeogenesis), some fat. Starvation state (>12 hours, prolonged): glycogen depleted (after ~18 hours), ketosis develops (after 2-3 days), gluconeogenesis continues (amino acids from muscle breakdown, lactate, glycerol), fat breakdown increases (primary energy source), protein sparing (ketones reduce glucose requirement, preserve muscle); energy source: fat (primary, ~70% calories), ketones (brain, heart, ~20%), glucose (from gluconeogenesis, ~10%). Exercise metabolism: duration and intensity determine fuel: short/high-intensity (anaerobic: glycogen, glucose, produces lactate), moderate/long-duration (aerobic: glucose, glycogen, fat, mixture depends on intensity, duration, fitness), prolonged (fat increasingly used, glycogen depleted, "hitting the wall" in endurance events).

Energy Balance and Metabolism:

  • •Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE): Total calories burned per day. Components: Basal metabolic rate BMR (~60-70% of TDEE: energy for basic body functions at rest: breathing, circulation, cell function, brain activity, organs, measured under strict conditions: awake, rest, fasting, neutral temperature, varies by: age, sex, body size, body composition, genetics, hormones), Thermic effect of food TEF (~10%: energy to digest, absorb, metabolize food, protein highest: 20-30% of calories, carbohydrates: 5-10%, fats: 0-3%, mixed meal: ~10%), Physical activity (~20-30%, varies widely: sedentary ~15%, active ~30%, athletes up to 50%+, includes: exercise, non-exercise activity thermogenesis NEAT: fidgeting, posture, daily activities, walking, standing). Calculating TDEE: BMR × activity factor (sedentary: 1.2, light activity: 1.375, moderate: 1.55, very active: 1.725, extra active: 1.9), or equations: Mifflin-St Jeor (most accurate for most people), Harris-Benedict (older, less accurate), or direct measurement: indirect calorimetry (respiratory gas analysis, measures O₂ consumption, CO₂ production).
  • •Energy Balance: Energy in = Energy out: weight maintenance. Energy in > Energy out: weight gain (stored as fat, muscle if resistance training + adequate protein). Energy in < Energy out: weight loss (fat loss, muscle loss if inadequate protein, excessive deficit). 1 pound fat = ~3500 kcal (deficit of 500 kcal/day = 1 lb/week loss, 250 kcal/day = 0.5 lb/week, more realistic, sustainable). Factors affecting metabolism: age (BMR decreases ~2-3% per decade after 20s, muscle loss, hormonal changes), sex (men: higher BMR, more muscle mass, larger body size), body composition (muscle: metabolically active, burns calories at rest, fat: less active, "muscle burns more calories than fat"), genetics (accounts for ~20-40% of variation in BMR, metabolism), hormones (thyroid: T3, T4, hypothyroidism: low BMR, hyperthyroidism: high BMR, menopause: decreased BMR, testosterone: increases BMR, muscle mass, growth hormone), sleep (adequate sleep: normal metabolism, poor sleep: decreased BMR, increased hunger hormones, increased cortisol), stress (chronic stress: increased cortisol, may affect metabolism, abdominal fat deposition), dieting (adaptive thermogenesis: BMR decreases during caloric restriction, "metabolic adaptation," may persist after weight loss, makes maintenance difficult, "yo-yo dieting" may worsen this).

Nutritional Assessment and Dietary Guidelines

Nutritional assessment evaluates nutritional status through dietary intake, anthropometric measurements, biochemical tests, and clinical examination. Dietary guidelines provide evidence-based recommendations for healthy eating patterns to prevent chronic diseases and promote optimal health. Understanding assessment methods and following dietary guidelines helps individuals and healthcare providers make informed decisions about nutrition.

Dietary Reference Intakes (DRIs):

  • •Estimated Average Requirement (EAR): Average daily intake estimated to meet requirement of 50% of healthy individuals in specific age/sex group, used to set RDA, basis for nutrient adequacy assessment.
  • •Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA): Average daily intake sufficient to meet requirement of 97-98% of healthy individuals in specific age/sex group, set at EAR + 2 standard deviations (SD), goal for individuals: meet or exceed RDA, safe for nearly all healthy people. Used for: planning diets, evaluating adequacy, food labeling, fortification.
  • •Adequate Intake (AI): Average daily intake level assumed to be adequate when RDA cannot be determined (insufficient evidence for EAR), based on observed or experimentally determined approximations, used when: RDA cannot be set, may be higher than RDA would be if known, goal: meet or exceed AI.
  • •Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL): Maximum daily intake unlikely to cause adverse health effects in nearly all individuals, applies to total intake from food, water, supplements, not a recommended intake (stay below UL), established for nutrients that can be toxic at high levels, varies by age/sex, not established for all nutrients (insufficient evidence). Examples: vitamin C: 2000mg/day (diarrhea, kidney stones), vitamin D: 4000 IU/day (100μg, hypercalcemia, kidney damage), calcium: 2500mg/day (kidney stones, hypercalcemia), iron: 45mg/day (constipation, organ damage), folate: 1000μg/day (masks B12 deficiency, may increase cancer risk).
  • •Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Ranges (AMDRs): Ranges of intake for macronutrients associated with reduced chronic disease risk while providing adequate essential nutrients. Carbohydrates: 45-65% of calories (for energy, fiber, essential), Proteins: 10-35% of calories (adequate for health, higher for athletes, muscle gain), Fats: 20-35% of calories (essential fatty acids, fat-soluble vitamins, higher for ketogenic diets, but concerns), saturated fat: <10% of calories (heart health).

Dietary Guidelines for Americans (2020-2025):

  • •Core Recommendations: Follow healthy eating pattern across lifespan (all food groups, variety, appropriate calories), customize healthy eating pattern to preferences, cultural traditions, budgets, focus on nutrient density (nutrients per calorie, choose nutrient-rich foods, limit empty calories), limit foods/beverages higher in added sugars, saturated fat, sodium (sugar: <10% calories, saturated fat: <10% calories, sodium: <2300mg/day), shift to healthier choices (replace processed with whole, replace sugary drinks with water, replace refined grains with whole grains), support healthy eating patterns for all (policies, programs, environments).
  • •Food Groups: Vegetables (2.5-3 cups/day: dark green, red/orange, legumes, starchy, other, variety of colors, forms: fresh, frozen, canned, limit sodium in canned, nutrient-dense), Fruits (1.5-2 cups/day: whole fruits preferred, fruit juice limited, variety, nutrient-dense), Grains (5-8 oz/day: at least half whole grains, whole wheat, brown rice, quinoa, oats, limit refined: white bread, white rice, pastries), Protein (5-6.5 oz/day: seafood, lean meats, poultry, eggs, legumes, nuts, seeds, soy products, variety, limit processed meats, choose lean), Dairy (3 cups/day: milk, yogurt, cheese, fortified soy milk, choose low-fat/non-fat, limit added sugars). Oils (27g/day: vegetable oils, nuts, seeds, avocados, limit solid fats: butter, lard, stick margarine).
  • •Limits: Added sugars: <10% calories (6 tsp/100 calories for 2000 kcal diet: 50g/200 kcal, average American: ~77g/308 kcal, excess), saturated fat: <10% calories (<22g for 2000 kcal diet, average: ~28g, excess), sodium: <2300mg/day (1 tsp salt, general), <1500mg/day (hypertension, African Americans, middle-aged/elderly), average: ~3400mg/day (excess), alcohol: moderation (1 drink/day women, 2 drinks/day men, 1 drink = 14g alcohol: 12 oz beer, 5 oz wine, 1.5 oz spirits).

MyPlate Guidelines:

  • •Visual Guide: Plate divided into sections: 50% fruits and vegetables (30% vegetables: variety of colors, 20% fruits: whole fruits preferred), 25% grains (at least half whole grains: whole wheat, brown rice, quinoa, oats, limit refined: white bread, white rice, pastries), 25% protein (seafood: 2 servings/week minimum, lean meats, poultry, eggs, legumes, nuts, seeds, soy products, variety, limit processed meats: bacon, sausage, hot dogs, deli meats), dairy on the side (low-fat/non-fat milk, yogurt, cheese, fortified plant milks, choose unsweetened). Oils: small amounts (liquid at room temperature: olive, canola, soybean, limit solid fats).
  • •Key Principles: Variety (eat different foods from each group, different colors, forms, nutrient diversity), Moderation (portion control, not eliminating foods, balance), Proportionality (right proportions of each group, visual guide: plate model, easier than counting calories), Nutrient density (choose nutrient-rich options, maximize nutrients per calorie, whole foods over processed), Personalization (adjust portions for age, sex, activity level, calorie needs, customize to preferences, cultural traditions).

Healthy Eating Patterns:

  • •Mediterranean Diet: Based on traditional diets of Mediterranean region (Greece, Italy, Spain). Characteristics: high in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds, olive oil (primary fat source, MUFAs), moderate in fish, seafood, poultry, dairy (yogurt, cheese), low in red meat, processed meats, sweets, red wine (moderation, optional). Benefits: reduced heart disease risk (PREDIMED study: ~30% reduction), improved cognitive function, reduced Alzheimer's risk, reduced cancer risk (some types), longevity, reduced inflammation, improved insulin sensitivity. Evidence: extensively researched, consistently associated with health benefits.
  • •DASH Diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension): Developed to lower blood pressure. Characteristics: high in fruits (4-5 servings), vegetables (4-5 servings), whole grains (6-8 servings), low-fat dairy (2-3 servings), lean proteins (fish, poultry, legumes, nuts: 4-6 oz), limited in sodium (<2300mg, ideally <1500mg for hypertension), saturated fat, sweets, red meat, added sugars. Benefits: lowers blood pressure (systolic: ~6-11 mmHg, diastolic: ~3-6 mmHg, comparable to medications), reduces heart disease risk, stroke risk, improves cholesterol, supports weight loss. Evidence: multiple studies, recommended for hypertension, general heart health.
  • •Vegetarian/Vegan Diets: Types: lacto-ovo vegetarian (dairy, eggs, no meat, fish, poultry), lacto-vegetarian (dairy, no eggs, meat, fish, poultry), ovo-vegetarian (eggs, no dairy, meat, fish, poultry), pescatarian (fish, seafood, no meat, poultry, may include dairy, eggs), vegan (no animal products: no meat, fish, poultry, dairy, eggs, honey). Benefits: lower heart disease risk, lower blood pressure, lower cholesterol, lower type 2 diabetes risk, lower cancer risk (some types), lower BMI, longer life expectancy, environmental benefits. Concerns: nutrient adequacy (B12: supplements required for vegans, fortified foods, iron: plant sources less absorbable, vitamin C enhances absorption, calcium: dairy alternative needed, vitamin D: supplements if limited sun, omega-3: ALA adequate for most, but EPA/DHA may need supplementation: algae-based, zinc: plant sources less absorbable, soaking/sprouting helps, protein: variety, complementary proteins not necessary at same meal). Well-planned vegetarian/vegan diets can meet all nutrient needs, recommended by major health organizations (ADA: American Dietetic Association, Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics position paper: appropriately planned vegetarian/vegan diets are healthful, nutritionally adequate, may provide health benefits).
  • •MIND Diet (Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay): Combines Mediterranean and DASH diets, focuses on brain health. Characteristics: emphasizes green leafy vegetables (6+ servings/week), other vegetables (1+ serving/day), berries (2+ servings/week), nuts (5+ servings/week), whole grains (3+ servings/day), fish (1+ serving/week), poultry (2+ servings/week), beans (3+ servings/week), olive oil (primary fat), wine (1 glass/day, optional), limits red meat (<4 servings/week), butter/margarine (<1 tbsp/day), cheese (<1 serving/week), pastries/sweets (<5 servings/week), fried foods (<1 serving/week). Benefits: reduced Alzheimer's risk (up to 53% reduction with strict adherence, ~35% with moderate adherence), slower cognitive decline, improved brain health. Evidence: observational studies, randomized controlled trials ongoing, promising for cognitive health.

Nutrition and Disease: Prevention and Management

Nutrition plays a crucial role in both preventing and managing chronic diseases. Poor nutrition contributes to numerous health problems, including cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, obesity, cancer, osteoporosis, and more. Conversely, healthy eating patterns can reduce disease risk, manage existing conditions, and improve quality of life. Understanding the relationship between nutrition and disease empowers individuals to make informed dietary choices for optimal health.

Cardiovascular Disease (CVD):

  • •Risk Factors and Prevention: Leading cause of death worldwide. Risk factors: high blood pressure (hypertension: DASH diet lowers BP, reduce sodium: <2300mg, ideally <1500mg, increase potassium: fruits, vegetables, DASH emphasizes), high cholesterol (LDL: "bad cholesterol," elevated increases risk, reduce saturated fat: <10% calories, <7% ideal, reduce trans fat: minimize, increase soluble fiber: oats, barley, legumes, apples, reduces LDL), low HDL (HDL: "good cholesterol," protective, exercise increases, moderate alcohol may increase, but not recommended for non-drinkers), triglycerides (elevated increases risk, reduce refined carbs, added sugars, alcohol, increase omega-3: fish, walnuts, flaxseeds), obesity (excess weight increases risk, weight loss reduces risk, Mediterranean, DASH support weight loss), diabetes (increases CVD risk, control blood glucose, Mediterranean, DASH improve insulin sensitivity), inflammation (chronic inflammation contributes, omega-3, antioxidants reduce inflammation, Mediterranean diet anti-inflammatory).
  • •Dietary Patterns for Heart Health: Mediterranean diet: ~30% reduction in heart disease risk (PREDIMED study), DASH diet: lowers blood pressure, cholesterol, reduces heart disease risk, both emphasize: fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, healthy fats (olive oil, omega-3), fish, limited red meat, processed foods, added sugars, sodium. Specific nutrients: omega-3 fatty acids (EPA, DHA: fish, 2 servings/week minimum, 250-500mg/day, reduce triglycerides, inflammation, arrhythmias, may reduce heart disease risk, death), fiber (soluble: reduces LDL, insoluble: promotes satiety, weight management, 25-38g/day recommended, DASH emphasizes), antioxidants (vitamin E, C, beta-carotene, polyphenols: fruits, vegetables, nuts, may reduce oxidative stress, inflammation, evidence mixed for supplements: food sources preferable), plant sterols/stanols (found in plant foods, supplements reduce LDL: margarines, juices, recommended for high cholesterol).
  • •Limits for Heart Health: Saturated fat: <10% calories (<7% ideal), sources: red meat, full-fat dairy, butter, tropical oils, replace with: unsaturated fats, lean proteins, low-fat dairy. Trans fat: minimize, ideally 0g, sources: partially hydrogenated oils (banned in US), processed foods, limit fried foods, baked goods. Sodium: <2300mg/day (<1500mg for hypertension, African Americans, middle-aged/elderly), sources: processed foods (75% of dietary sodium), table salt, restaurant meals, reduce: read labels, cook at home, use herbs/spices, choose fresh foods. Added sugars: <10% calories, <25g/day women, <36g/day men, sources: soft drinks, sweets, processed foods, reduce: limit sugary drinks, choose whole fruits, read labels. Alcohol: moderation (1 drink/day women, 2 men, excess increases CVD risk, hypertension, arrhythmias).

Type 2 Diabetes:

  • •Prevention: Risk factors: obesity (major risk factor, 80-90% of type 2 diabetes cases, weight loss: 5-7% body weight reduces risk by ~58%, Diabetes Prevention Program DPP study), physical inactivity (exercise improves insulin sensitivity, 150 minutes/week moderate activity reduces risk), poor diet (high refined carbs, added sugars, processed foods, low fiber, excess calories increase risk), genetics (family history increases risk, but lifestyle modifiable). Prevention strategies: weight loss (modest: 5-10% body weight), physical activity (150 minutes/week moderate, or 75 minutes vigorous), healthy eating (Mediterranean, DASH, focus on: whole grains, fiber, fruits, vegetables, lean proteins, healthy fats, limit: refined carbs, added sugars, processed foods, portion control).
  • •Management: Goals: maintain blood glucose in target range (HbA1c <7%, individual targets vary), prevent complications (heart disease, kidney disease, neuropathy, retinopathy, foot problems), maintain quality of life. Carbohydrate management: counting carbs (standard: 45-60g per meal, varies by individual, medications, activity), glycemic index (choose low-GI foods: whole grains, legumes, most fruits, vegetables, limit high-GI: white bread, white rice, sweets), fiber (soluble: slows glucose absorption, improves blood glucose, LDL, recommended: 25-38g/day, fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes), timing (consistent meal times, spacing meals: 4-6 hours apart, avoid skipping meals, prevents hypoglycemia, hyperglycemia). Protein: adequate intake (0.8-1.0g/kg, higher if active, older, supports muscle mass, satiety, blood glucose stability), quality sources (lean meats, fish, poultry, legumes, nuts, dairy, variety). Fat: healthy fats (unsaturated: olive oil, nuts, seeds, avocados, omega-3: fish, may improve insulin sensitivity, reduce inflammation), limit saturated/trans fats (heart disease risk higher in diabetes). Meal planning: plate method (1/2 non-starchy vegetables, 1/4 lean protein, 1/4 whole grains/starchy vegetables), carbohydrate counting (learn to estimate, read labels, measure portions, work with dietitian), exchange lists (similar foods grouped, facilitate meal planning). Monitoring: blood glucose testing (self-monitoring, patterns, adjustments), HbA1c (3-month average, target <7%, but individualized), continuous glucose monitoring (CGM, real-time data, trends, better management).

Osteoporosis:

  • •Prevention and Management: Bone disease: low bone density, increased fracture risk, especially hip, spine, wrist, common in postmenopausal women (estrogen protects bone, loss after menopause accelerates loss), elderly, men also affected (later in life). Risk factors: inadequate calcium (peak bone mass: maximum bone density achieved by ~30 years, adequate calcium during childhood, adolescence, young adulthood crucial, maintain through life), vitamin D deficiency (required for calcium absorption, bone mineralization, optimal levels: 30-50 ng/mL, sunlight, supplements, fortified foods), inadequate protein (bone structure, muscle mass, falls prevention), smoking (reduces bone density), excessive alcohol (interferes with calcium absorption, bone formation), sedentary lifestyle (weight-bearing exercise: walking, running, dancing, resistance training, builds bone density, prevents loss), genetics, medications (glucocorticoids, some antacids reduce absorption), hormonal factors (low estrogen, testosterone).
  • •Nutrients for Bone Health: Calcium: RDA: 1000-1300mg/day (varies by age/sex, highest: adolescents 1300mg, pregnant/lactating, postmenopausal women, elderly), sources: dairy (highest bioavailability: milk, yogurt, cheese), fortified foods (orange juice, plant milks, cereals), green leafy vegetables (broccoli, kale, bok choy, spinach: oxalates reduce absorption, but still contribute), fish with bones (sardines, salmon), tofu (calcium-set), almonds, sesame seeds. Absorption: enhanced by vitamin D, acidic environment, lactose (infants), reduced by oxalates, phytates, excessive fiber, tannins, excessive protein/sodium (increases urinary calcium loss, limit to RDA, not excessive). Timing: distributed throughout day (better absorption than large single dose), with meals (enhances absorption), supplements: calcium carbonate (most common, requires stomach acid, take with meals, cheaper), calcium citrate (better absorbed, doesn't require acid, take with/without meals, better for elderly, acid blockers). Vitamin D: required for calcium absorption (without adequate D, only ~10-15% calcium absorbed, with adequate D: ~30-40% absorbed), bone mineralization, muscle function (reduces falls risk, fractures). RDA: 600-800 IU/day (15-20μg), higher for elderly (800 IU, 20μg), optimal blood levels: 30-50 ng/mL (75-125 nmol/L). Sources: sunlight (primary source in most people, 10-15 minutes arms/face, 3x/week, varies by: latitude, season, time of day, skin pigmentation, age, sunscreen, cloud cover, limited in winter at high latitudes), fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, tuna, sardines, cod liver oil), fortified foods (milk, orange juice, cereals, some plant milks), egg yolks, mushrooms (UV-exposed), supplements (D3 cholecalciferol preferred, more effective than D2). Magnesium: bone structure, enzyme function, vitamin D activation (required for conversion to active form), RDA: 310-420mg/day, sources: green leafy vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds, dark chocolate, fish. Vitamin K: bone metabolism (carboxylates osteocalcin, bone mineralization, may reduce fracture risk), RDA: 90-120μg/day, sources: green leafy vegetables (kale, spinach, broccoli), fermented foods (natto highest), dairy, meat, eggs, gut bacteria synthesis. Protein: adequate intake (0.8-1.0g/kg, higher if active, older: 1.0-1.2g/kg, prevents sarcopenia: age-related muscle loss, falls, fractures), sources: lean meats, fish, poultry, legumes, nuts, dairy, variety. Phosphorus: bone structure (with calcium, 85% in bones/teeth), RDA: 700mg/day, sources: protein-rich foods (widespread, deficiency rare, but balance with calcium important, excessive phosphorus: soft drinks, processed foods, may interfere with calcium absorption, bone health).
  • •Lifestyle Factors: Weight-bearing exercise: walking, jogging, running, dancing, tennis, jumping, resistance training (weights, resistance bands, bodyweight), builds bone density, prevents loss, 30 minutes/day, 3-5 days/week, start gradually, consult healthcare provider if osteoporosis, falls risk. Avoid: smoking (reduces bone density, interferes with calcium absorption, bone formation), excessive alcohol (interferes with calcium absorption, bone formation, increases falls risk, limit: 1 drink/day women, 2 men), excessive caffeine (may increase urinary calcium loss, but moderate intake: 1-2 cups/day, probably fine, especially with adequate calcium), excessive sodium (increases urinary calcium loss, limit to <2300mg/day, <1500mg if hypertension). Prevention across lifespan: childhood/adolescence (build peak bone mass, adequate calcium: 1300mg/day, vitamin D, physical activity), young adulthood (maintain bone mass, adequate nutrition, exercise), middle age (slow loss, adequate nutrition, exercise), postmenopause (accelerated loss, adequate calcium: 1200mg/day, vitamin D: 800 IU/day, exercise, may need medications: bisphosphonates, hormone therapy, calcitonin, but discuss risks/benefits with healthcare provider), elderly (maintain bone density, prevent falls: muscle strength, balance, vision, medications review, adequate nutrition: protein, calcium, vitamin D, may need supplements, consult healthcare provider).

Cancer:

  • •Prevention: Risk factors: diet (estimated 30-40% of cancers may be preventable through diet, lifestyle), obesity (increases risk: breast, colorectal, endometrial, kidney, pancreatic, esophageal, liver, gallbladder cancers, weight loss reduces risk), alcohol (increases risk: breast, colorectal, liver, esophageal, head/neck cancers, no "safe" level, but risk increases with amount, limit or avoid), processed meats (increases colorectal cancer risk, limit: bacon, sausage, hot dogs, deli meats, <18 oz/week, or avoid), red meat (high intake may increase colorectal cancer risk, limit: <18 oz/week, choose lean cuts, avoid charring: grilling, creates carcinogens, marinate, precook, flip frequently), excess calories (may increase risk through obesity), low fiber (may increase colorectal cancer risk, adequate fiber: 25-38g/day, whole grains, fruits, vegetables, legumes).
  • •Protective Factors: Fruits and vegetables: antioxidants (vitamin C, E, beta-carotene, lycopene, lutein: may reduce oxidative damage, DNA damage, cancer risk), phytochemicals (plant compounds: flavonoids, polyphenols, carotenoids, indoles, glucosinolates: may have anti-cancer properties, reduce inflammation, DNA damage, cell proliferation, promote apoptosis: programmed cell death), fiber (may reduce colorectal cancer risk: binds carcinogens, reduces transit time, promotes healthy gut microbiome, produces SCFAs: butyrate may reduce cancer risk). Recommended: 5+ servings/day (variety of colors, forms: fresh, frozen, canned, limit sodium in canned), specific: cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, Brussels sprouts: glucosinolates, indoles, may reduce cancer risk), tomatoes (lycopene: prostate cancer protection, better absorbed cooked, with fat), berries (anthocyanins, ellagic acid: antioxidants, may reduce cancer risk), citrus fruits (vitamin C, flavonoids: may reduce cancer risk), dark leafy greens (folate, carotenoids, fiber: may reduce cancer risk). Whole grains: fiber, phytochemicals, vitamins, minerals, may reduce colorectal cancer risk, recommended: at least half of grains whole (whole wheat, brown rice, quinoa, oats, barley). Legumes: fiber, phytochemicals, folate, may reduce cancer risk, recommended: 2-3 servings/week (beans, lentils, chickpeas, soy: may reduce breast cancer risk, but evidence mixed). Fish: omega-3 fatty acids (EPA, DHA: may reduce inflammation, some cancer risk, but evidence mixed), recommended: 2 servings/week minimum (fatty fish: salmon, mackerel, sardines, tuna). Green tea: catechins (EGCG: epigallocatechin gallate, antioxidant, may reduce cancer risk, but evidence mixed), may have protective effects, but not a substitute for overall healthy diet. Limiting alcohol: reduces risk of multiple cancers, no "safe" level, but risk increases with amount, limit or avoid (if drink: 1 drink/day women, 2 men maximum, but less is better). Maintaining healthy weight: reduces risk of multiple cancers (breast: postmenopausal, colorectal, endometrial, kidney, pancreatic, esophageal, liver, gallbladder), weight loss if overweight/obese (modest: 5-10% body weight, gradual: 1-2 lbs/week, sustainable).
  • •During Cancer Treatment: Nutrition support: maintain weight, muscle mass (cachexia: cancer-related weight loss, muscle loss, worsens prognosis, quality of life), manage side effects (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, taste changes, mouth sores: affects appetite, intake), support immune function (adequate protein, vitamins, minerals), energy for healing (adequate calories, but quality important: nutrient-dense foods). Recommendations: work with registered dietitian (specialized in oncology nutrition, individualized meal plan, addresses side effects, medications, treatments), small, frequent meals (if appetite poor, nausea), nutrient-dense foods (maximize nutrition in smaller amounts: smoothies, shakes, if appetite poor), adequate protein (preserve muscle mass: 1.2-1.5g/kg if possible, higher if active, older, but may be difficult with side effects), hydration (adequate fluids, especially with diarrhea, vomiting, may need electrolyte replacement), food safety (immunocompromised: wash hands, cook thoroughly, avoid raw foods: sushi, raw eggs, unpasteurized dairy, soft cheeses, deli meats, may need to avoid), supplements (may be needed if deficiencies, but discuss with healthcare team, some supplements may interfere with treatments, high-dose antioxidants: controversial during treatment, may protect cancer cells, discuss with oncologist).

Obesity and Weight Management:

  • •Weight Loss Strategies: Energy balance: energy in < energy out (caloric deficit: 500 kcal/day = 1 lb/week loss, 250 kcal/day = 0.5 lb/week, more realistic, sustainable, ~1% body weight/week maximum, faster may cause muscle loss, metabolic adaptation, difficult to maintain). Diet quality: nutrient-dense foods (fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, healthy fats, maximize nutrition per calorie, satiety), limit empty calories (refined carbs, added sugars, processed foods, sugary drinks: high calories, low nutrition, poor satiety, contribute to weight gain, soft drinks: liquid calories, poor satiety, major contributor to obesity). Portion control: smaller portions (use smaller plates, bowls, measure portions, read labels, restaurant portions: often 2-3 servings, take home half, share), mindful eating (eat slowly, savor food, recognize hunger/satiety cues, avoid distractions: TV, phone, prevents overeating). Meal timing: regular meals (prevents extreme hunger, overeating, maintains metabolism, blood glucose, 3 meals/day, or smaller meals + snacks: 4-6 times/day, whatever works for individual), breakfast (may help weight management, but not essential: individual preference, if hungry, eat, if not, skip, but don't compensate later), late-night eating (may contribute to weight gain: circadian rhythms, metabolism, but evidence mixed, if hungry, eat, but choose nutrient-dense, avoid large meals close to bedtime).
  • •Popular Diets: Low-carb diets (Atkins, ketogenic: <50g carbs/day, very high fat: 70-80% calories, may promote weight loss initially: water loss, reduced appetite, but long-term: similar to other diets when calories controlled, concerns: sustainability, nutrient adequacy, cardiovascular effects, kidney function, evidence mixed, not superior for weight loss long-term when calories controlled, but may work for some individuals, consult healthcare provider). Low-fat diets (historically popular: <30% calories from fat, may reduce calorie intake, but may reduce satiety, limit nutrient absorption, not superior to other diets when calories controlled, quality of fats matters more than quantity). Mediterranean diet (associated with weight loss, improved health, sustainable, nutrient-dense, recommended). DASH diet (lowers blood pressure, supports weight loss, recommended). Intermittent fasting (time-restricted eating: 16:8, 18:6, alternate-day fasting: may reduce calories, improve insulin sensitivity, but not superior when calories controlled, sustainability concerns, individual preference, may work for some, but not magic: still requires caloric deficit, consult healthcare provider if diabetes, medications, medical conditions). Plant-based diets (vegetarian, vegan: lower BMI, associated with weight loss, health benefits, but not magic: still need caloric deficit, can overeat plant foods, but generally more nutrient-dense, lower calorie density, higher fiber, satiety, recommended). Best diet: whatever individual can stick to long-term, creates caloric deficit, nutrient-dense, sustainable, healthy, consult registered dietitian for personalized plan.
  • •Weight Maintenance: After weight loss: 80-95% regain weight within 5 years (metabolic adaptation: BMR decreases, persists after weight loss, makes maintenance difficult, hunger hormones increase, satiety hormones decrease), successful maintainers: regular physical activity (60-90 minutes/day moderate activity, or 30-45 minutes vigorous, most important factor for maintenance), continued dietary monitoring (self-monitoring: food diary, weigh regularly, but not obsessively, adjust as needed), breakfast (many maintainers eat breakfast, but not universal), consistent eating patterns (regular meals, similar calories most days, but flexibility important, can enjoy treats in moderation), high dietary restraint (but not too restrictive: allows flexibility, treats, prevents deprivation, binging), support systems (family, friends, support groups, accountability, social support). Strategies: continue healthy habits (don't return to old habits after reaching goal, maintain new lifestyle, not temporary "diet"), regular physical activity (crucial for maintenance, preserves muscle mass, increases BMR, energy expenditure), continued self-monitoring (weigh regularly: weekly, not daily, adjust as needed, food diary if helpful, but not forever: develop intuitive eating, recognize hunger/satiety cues), flexibility (can enjoy treats, special occasions, but return to routine, balance: 80% healthy, 20% treats, or individual preference), plan for setbacks (weight fluctuates: 2-5 lbs normal, holidays, vacations, stress, don't panic, return to routine, don't give up), long-term mindset (lifestyle change, not temporary diet, permanent changes, but gradual, sustainable).
